# Bellingham Public Schools Budget Trends: 2024–2025

## Research Prompt

What are the trends you notice around budget planning year over year between 2024 and 2025? Where are the areas of growth and which areas see the steepest cuts?

## Executive Summary

The Bellingham Public Schools district has entered the 2024–2025 budget cycle facing significant fiscal pressure due to inadequate state-level funding, inflationary pressures, and structural deficiencies in the school funding model. Throughout 2024 and into early 2025, the district emphasized grant acquisition and cost containment, while highlighting growing concerns about systemic underfunding—particularly in Special Education, MSOCs (materials, supplies, and operating costs), and transportation.

The district reports a **"hold steady"** approach for the 2024–25 school year, but acknowledges deeper budget cuts may be required in 2025–26 unless state funding substantially improves. Notably, the district saw record grant revenues in 2023–24 and is leveraging attrition and internal efficiencies to avoid more severe service disruptions.

## Key Budget Trends

### 1. **Worsening State-Level Funding Landscape**

Multiple meetings underscore the state's failure to keep up with inflation and the needs of local districts:

- On **January 16, 2025**, Superintendent Baker reported:  
  > “Key budget challenges include the state not fully funding basic education due to inflation, the state’s regionalization formula, staff experience factors and the state COLA/IPD not being funded.”  
  > “The percentage of the state’s budget designated to K-12 education has decreased in past years.”

- On **April 17, 2024**, Baker indicated:  
  > “The percentage of K-12 education funding in the state’s operating budget has decreased over time... it’s expected that 2024–25 will be a 'hold steady' year for the budget, other than salaries.”

These concerns were repeatedly echoed, with board members noting that systemic fixes must occur at the state level.

### 2. **Efforts to Reduce Expenditures Through Attrition and Efficiency**

- As early as **July 18, 2024**, it was clear that budget constraints were driving difficult decisions:  
  > “City of Bellingham and Whatcom County leadership shared this week that they are facing budget challenges... It has been recommended... that districts... have common talking points, which include fully funding special education, pupil transportation and MSOCs.”

- In **February 2025**, the district reaffirmed its response to financial strain:  
  > “District staff continue to take steps to reduce our budget, including attrition and strategic work with labor groups and the Bellingham Public Schools Foundation.”

### 3. **Increased Grant Funding as a Lifeline**

The district has achieved record-breaking success in securing grant funding to backfill gaps:

- On **January 16, 2025**, the board celebrated:  
  > “The 23–24 school year recorded the highest level of financial receipts from grant funding on record.”  
  > “Grants such as the financial literacy grant, bike education grant, and funding that supports our first school-based health center... will help our students in the future.”

- On **January 18, 2024**, board members noted:  
  > “Record number of grants applied for and received!”  
  > “The total number of submitted grant applications and number received reflect a substantial increase over the year prior.”

Grants helped to sustain programs including the Central Kitchen, hearing loss testing, and school-based health initiatives.

### 4. **Capital Investments Continue Amid Operational Constraints**

Despite general budgetary strain, the district moved forward with selective capital improvements:

- On **July 18, 2024**, Superintendent Baker outlined capital spending:  
  > “The award of $2.7 million for solar arrays at five schools.”  
  > “Replacement of boilers and water heaters at Bellingham High School.”

- A resolution passed that same day authorized the **construction of the Community Transitions building** and a **purchase and sale agreement for property** at 1835 Barkley Blvd.

This demonstrates a strategic focus on infrastructure improvements even amid operating budget challenges.

### 5. **Ongoing Advocacy and Transparent Planning**

The board and superintendent maintained a strong emphasis on transparent planning and state-level advocacy:

- According to **January 2025 board minutes**:  
  > “Preparations for the 2024–25 (as well as the upcoming 2025–26) budget have been commendable for the degree of transparency, communication and multi-channeled approach to communicating budget challenges and plans.”

- At multiple meetings, including **June 18, 2024**, it was noted that:  
  > “Each day, there are reports of districts across the state facing difficult decisions related to budget reductions for the 2024–25 school year.”

## Areas of Growth

- **Grant Acquisition**: Highest level of grant funding recorded in 2023–24.
- **Capital Projects**: Solar energy expansion, boiler replacement, and facility construction continue.
- **Community Partnerships**: Continued investment in programs like Community Transitions and Options High School’s health center.

## Areas of Decline

- **General Operating Budget**: Flat or reduced funding levels noted due to state inaction.
- **Programmatic Reductions**: The BEATS program ended in 2024 due to lost funding.
- **Structural Funding Challenges**: Local levies remain overburdened by costs that should be state-funded (e.g., MSOCs, Special Ed, transportation).

> “The legislature has so far focused on Special Education, MSOCs and transportation. It’s expected that a formal budget will be recommended to the board for adoption in August.”  
> —June 18, 2024

## Conclusion

Bellingham Public Schools enters the 2025–26 planning cycle with a disciplined, transparent approach but remains deeply constrained by the shortcomings of Washington State’s school funding model. Through increased grant funding, strategic capital investments, and strong fiscal stewardship, the district has managed to avoid drastic programmatic cuts thus far—but the warning signs for future austerity remain clear unless structural reforms are enacted at the state level.
