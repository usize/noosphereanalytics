Executive Summary

Throughout 2024, school districts across Washington State were heavily engaged in planning for and implementing significant budget reductions for the 2024‑25 school year, largely in response to the expiration of federal ESSER funds and ongoing enrollment declines. Blaine School District , for example, acknowledged the need for “significant budget reductions in the coming school year” in January 2024 (2024‑01‑22), and later estimated that “$2.7 million will be needed” in reductions for 2024‑2025, emphasizing the crucial role of the local levy (2024‑02‑20). Washougal School District similarly stated in January 2024 their requirement for “$3 million in reductions for the 2024‑25 school year” (2024‑01‑23). Puyallup School District ’s board directed the superintendent in February 2024 to make reductions to develop a balanced budget for 2024‑25 (2024‑02‑20). This period was marked by concrete steps to address anticipated deficits, including identifying positions previously covered by ESSER funding that would need to shift to local sources or be eliminated, as seen in Clarkston School District in August 2024 (2024‑08‑26). Some districts were also operationalising spending controls, such as budget freezes for the 2024‑25 fiscal year (Pasco School District , 2025‑03‑11; 2025‑02‑25).

As the calendar turned to 2025 and the focus shifted towards planning for the 2025‑26 school year, the landscape remained fiscally constrained. Districts continued to grapple with the impacts of rising operational costs and uncertainty regarding state funding levels. Renton School District ’s budget planning for 2025‑26 in March 2025 included proposed total reductions to ensure financial stability (2025‑03‑26). Dieringer School District reviewed their preliminary 2025‑26 budget calculations in January 2025, which showed “a need to reduce expenditures by 1.6 million in order to build a balanced budget” (2025‑01‑27). Concerns about insufficient state funding for areas like Special Education and Maintenance, Supplies, and Operating Costs (MSOC) persisted into 2025 discussions (Edmonds School District , 2025‑03‑25; Puyallup School District , 2024‑02‑05). Edmonds School District noted in March 2025 that they were considering “extreme cuts… due to insufficient funding” (2025‑03‑11). The long‑term trend of declining enrollment, a statewide phenomenon since 2019‑20 (Clarkston School District , 2024‑08‑26), continued to inform staffing and budget adjustments for 2025‑26 (Pasco School District , 2025‑02‑25). Planning for local levies remained a critical component of future revenue strategies (Pasco School District , 2025‑02‑25).

In summary, the transition from planning for 2024‑25 to forecasting for 2025‑26 highlights a continuity of significant fiscal pressure on school districts. While 2024 saw districts finalising and implementing specific reduction plans driven by immediate challenges like ESSER expiration and realised enrollment drops, 2025 discussions reveal that these pressures are expected to continue, requiring ongoing efforts to manage expenditures, address rising costs, and navigate the uncertainties of state funding and local levy capacity.
