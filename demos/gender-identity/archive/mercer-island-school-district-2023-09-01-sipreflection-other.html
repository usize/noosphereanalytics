Islander Middle School Reflection on the 2023-2024
School Improvement Plan (SIP EES Data)

School Vision & Mission: Anchoring Our Work in 2023-2024

At Islander Middle School, we believe in preparing students to navigate an increasingly
complex world by combining rigorous academics with the social-emotional supports they
need to thrive. The 2023-2024 School Improvement Plan was guided by this mission, with a
focus on fostering community, promoting growth, and building transparent communication
with all stakeholders.

Our overarching goal last year was to build a school environment where every student and
staff member felt a strong sense of belonging, was excited to learn, and had clear,
supportive communication between staff, students, and families. This work was shaped by
the 4Bs adopted from Dr. Tammy Campbell—Belonging, Beliefs, Barriers, and
Broadcasting—and integrated the principles of Grading for Equity (G4 E) to ensure that
assessments were fair and transparent, supporting student growth rather than punitive
practices.

Goal 1: Fostering a Warm and Welcoming School Community (Belonging)

Our efforts to foster a warm and welcoming school community focused on reducing the
staff reported"I-they" gap, promoting respect, and increasing inclusivity. Significant strides
were made in creating a culture where students feel valued and heard, as evidenced by
improvements in student and staff survey data. Teachers also reported better respect and
understanding, particularly through professional development aimed at equity and
social-emotional learning (SEL).

e Staff Willingness to Work at Change: The gap between openness to change and
willingness to be held accountable decreased significantly.
o Data: The gap in willingness to change reduced from 23% to 7%, and
openness to new ideas improved from a 22% gap to a 12% gap.
e Students Feeling Cared For: Student survey results indicated that more students
felt they had an adult who cared about them.


o Data: 85% of students reported having an adult who knows and cares about
them (up from 84% the previous year).
e Teacher Practices Around Equity: Teacher surveys also reflected improvement in
the incorporation of SEL and equity practices in classrooms.
o Data: 80% of staff reported effective equity practices, up from 65% the
previous year.

How did we implement it?

We took a multifaceted approach to building a warm and welcoming school community. By
leveraging Relationship Circles, embedding SEL practices in daily instruction, and
focusing on equity-driven professional development, we were able to make a significant
impact on the school culture.

For example:

e Relationship Circles: These were introduced to foster communication and trust
within classrooms and among staff. Teacher Alexis Guerriero modeled the use of
relationship circles during classroom sessions, showing teachers how to facilitate
conversations that build empathy and respect. This helped students feel more
connected to their peers and teachers, ultimately promoting a stronger sense of
community.

However, there was a gap in the data collection surrounding the effectiveness of
these circles. Although many teachers found them useful, the school lacked concrete
tracking on how many classrooms regularly utilized the practice and what topics
were being discussed during these circles. Moving forward, we need to establish
better monitoring systems to ensure that these efforts are yielding the intended
outcomes.

e Student Recognition Programs: We recognized that students needed to feel valued
not just academically but holistically. In response, we implemented Student
Recognition programs, where students were acknowledged for their positive
behaviors, contributions, and achievements.

o For example: Principal feedback, gathered through student surveys and
Gator Time meetings, helped shape the system for recognizing students’
efforts beyond just their academic performance. By recognizing students
publicly, through morning announcements and at lunches,, we fostered an
environment where students felt seen and appreciated.

e Restorative Practices for Red Heart Students: Red Heart students were
identified as those needing extra attention and encouragement from teachers—this


could include students who were shy, struggling with phone addiction, or needing
other forms of support outside of formal IEP or 504 plans. We applied gentle
restorative practices to help these students navigate their challenges ina
non-punitive way. Teachers worked with these students to develop strategies that
promoted better habits and problem-solving skills, strengthening their sense of
belonging.

For example: In cases where students struggled with technology addiction,
restorative conversations between teachers and students helped set manageable
boundaries. By addressing these issues directly, students were empowered to take
ownership of their behaviors in a supportive environment.

Equity and SEL Practices: Throughout the year, we focused on increasing
equity-driven professional development. This included training staff on
social-emotional learning (SEL) and integrating these practices into daily
instruction. Teachers began using SEL-focused strategies to better connect with
students, such as morning check-ins and emotional regulation activities.

For example: By embedding these practices in their routines, teachers noticed an
improvement in classroom dynamics—students were more willing to engage in
discussions and communicate their needs. Teachers were able to use these
strategies to create spaces where all students felt heard, which contributed to
improved relationships between staff and students.

Addressing Hallway Behavior: As part of our commitment to building a respectful
school environment, we addressed the increasing concerns around hallway
behavior. A new Hallpass System was implemented to help track student movement
during class transitions, reducing hallway congestion and ensuring student
accountability.

For example: We introduced a Neighborhood Strategy Session, where teachers
and staff worked collaboratively to identify key behavioral challenges in the
hallways and brainstormed proactive strategies for addressing them. Additionally, a
new bell system was introduced, providing students with a one-minute warning
before the transition period ended, which helped minimize tardiness and improve
overall hallway behavior.

Community-Wide Sessions on Equity and Inclusion: To further support our
equity goals, we invited Hilary Bernstein (from the ADL) to lead a session in August
with our staff on modern Antisemitism and its impacts in our schools and
classrooms. We also invited Dr. Asaka to lead a session on Asian American
discrimination and how to be an effective ally. These sessions were part of our
broader initiative to promote inclusivity and cultural competence within our school.
For example: During these sessions, staff engaged in open dialogue about the
challenges faced by marginalized communities. The sessions helped increase
awareness of how to support one another and led to deeper discussions in


classrooms about equity and justice. This was an important step in fostering a
school culture where diversity is valued and all students feel included.

Our Effect:

e EES Staff Data: "My professional learning community work results in improved
student learning" increased from 79% to 90%.

e Reflection: This growth in PLC effectiveness reflected how collaboration,
recognition, and proactive support systems fostered a stronger sense of belonging
among both staff and students. However, gaps in tracking the implementation of
relationship circles and other initiatives will guide improvements in our data
collection for 2024-2025.

Goal 2: Igniting the Joy of Learning and Promoting Growth in Our
Community (Beliefs/Barriers)

In 2023-2024, we worked to build a growth-oriented culture by focusing on leadership
development, data-driven instruction, and student ownership of learning. Leadership
cultivation and intentional data use were key drivers of success. This goal centered on
promoting academic excellence while reducing the need for academic or behavioral
interventions. A core component was increasing recognition of student successes, and we
saw substantial growth in this area.

e Celebrating Student Success: We increased the number of recognition events and
moments for celebrating student successes.

o Data: The system for celebrating student success improved from 53% to
85%.

e Early Interventions for Struggling Students: Our efforts to implement an effective
Student Support Team (SST) model were successful, with early interventions
becoming a key focus.

o Data: 89% of staff reported that assessment data were being used to identify
student needs and provide appropriate interventions (up from 59% the prior

year).

How did we implement it?


We applied a structured approach to celebrating academic growth and providing timely
interventions for students who were struggling. This required a focus on Mindset 2,
data-driven decision-making, and building a school culture where students were
recognized for their efforts and given support when needed.

For example:

e Recognition Systems: We recognized that students were motivated not only by
academic success but also by being celebrated for their personal growth, positive
behaviors, and other contributions. To encourage this, we implemented regular
recognition opportunities for students.

o For example: We provided students positive praise through recognition
tickets. Each Monday we drew student names, read them over the
announcements and they had the opportunity to choose between a myriad of
potential rewards.

e Building a Supportive Testing Culture: During the May 1 District-Directed Day,
our focus was on building a supportive testing culture, emphasizing the
importance of helping students navigate assessments in a way that minimized
anxiety and maximized confidence.

o For example: Teachers were trained to help students develop growth
mindsets around testing, teaching them to view assessments as
opportunities to demonstrate progress rather than as high-stakes events.
This approach, rooted in Mindset 2, ensured that students felt supported
rather than pressured, and it promoted a positive attitude toward learning
and growth. As a result, we saw improved student engagement with
assessments and reduced test-related anxiety.

e Leadership Cultivation: As part of our leadership development efforts, we focused
on building the capacity of both staff and students to take ownership of their
learning and work collaboratively. The Book Study "The Art of Coaching Teams"
(Year 2) was an integral part of this work.

o For example: In our Building Cabinet, staff engaged in deep discussions
about how to apply coaching techniques within their teams. These
discussions emphasized the importance of leadership within professional
learning communities (PLCs) and helped cultivate leaders who could drive
growth in their respective departments. This work led to increased
collaboration across teams, fostering an environment where staff felt
empowered to lead change in their classrooms and contribute to the overall
academic growth of their students.

e Data-Driven Early Interventions: We recognized the importance of using data
proactively to identify struggling students early and provide targeted support. Our


Student Support Team (SST) became the backbone of these interventions, helping
us identify students who needed additional academic or behavioral assistance and
ensuring that interventions were put in place quickly.

o For example: We integrated iReady data and SBAC results into our SST
model, using this data to assess student needs and plan interventions. PLCs
regularly reviewed data on student progress, ensuring that interventions
were not only timely but also based on accurate, actionable data. This
resulted in a more efficient and effective intervention process, with struggling
students receiving support earlier, which helped prevent more significant
issues from developing.

e Mindset 2: Aligning Instruction and Assessment: Throughout the year, we
worked to align instruction and assessment practices across the school, ensuring
that our strategies for student growth were consistent and effective. Teachers
engaged in PLC discussions to calibrate assessments and instructional strategies,
ensuring that all students were given the opportunity to demonstrate their learning.

o For example: PLC teams met regularly to discuss questions 3 and 4: What
happens when students don’t learn? What happens when students have
already learned? These conversations led to the development of
differentiated strategies that addressed the needs of both struggling students
and those ready for more advanced challenges. This intentional focus on
student growth drove the improvement in early intervention data, with staff
becoming more adept at identifying and addressing learning gaps.

e Mickey Rowe Guest Speaker Event: A key highlight in fostering a growth mindset
and promoting inclusivity was the Belonging and Barriers Assembly featuring
Mickey Rowe, a guest speaker who shared his experiences as an individual with
disabilities. Mickey spoke about how access and equitable opportunities have
been critical to his success, underscoring the importance of providing all students
with the support they need to succeed.

o For example: Mickey's message resonated with both students and staff,
reinforcing the importance of breaking down barriers and ensuring that
everyone, regardless of ability, has the tools to thrive. This event also
provided staff with an opportunity to reflect on how they could better
support their own students, particularly those facing challenges due to
disabilities or other barriers.

Our Effect:


e EES Staff Data: "Staff 1 work with take responsibility for ensuring each student
learns in our school" increased from 86% to 91%.

e Reflection: Our efforts to align data use, cultivate leadership, and emphasize
student success were instrumental in increasing staff accountability for student
learning outcomes. The discussions during PLCs about student growth and
differentiation contributed to the rise in early intervention effectiveness. The
combination of recognition, data-driven decision-making, and leadership cultivation
set the stage for sustained growth in student achievement moving forward.

Goal 3: Building Bridges of Understanding through Effective
Communication (Broadcasting)

In 2023-2024, we focused on improving communication between the school, families, and
students. While we made notable strides in certain areas, challenges around clarity in
homework expectations and progress monitoring remained, and we identified areas for
further improvement as we moved into 2024-2025.

e Parent Involvement: We saw positive engagement through our partnerships with
the PTSA and Parent Advisory Committee, which allowed us to gather input and
share important information with families. These partnerships became a crucial
channel for discussing school priorities, student learning, and community events.

o Data: 81% of staff reported that the school encouraged parent involvement,
up from 74% the previous year.

e Communication Effectiveness: While we made some progress, communication
with families about homework policies and student progress remained a challenge.

o Data: Family satisfaction with communication effectiveness improved from
57% to 60%, but feedback indicated that more consistent and clear
messaging around homework was needed.

How did we implement it?

Our efforts centered around engaging with families through existing structures, improving
how we communicated learning, and identifying better platforms for promoting school
events and student success.

For example:


e Partnering with the PTSA and Parent Advisory Committee and IMS Parent
Affinity Groups: Instead of holding formal family focus groups, we worked closely
with our PTSA and Parent Advisory Committee to strengthen family involvement.
In addition, IMS implemented Parent Affinity group meetings throughout the year to
give us a better understand of the needs of our community. These groups provided a
forum for parents to voice concerns, share ideas, and engage with the school about
key issues.

o For example: Discussions during PTSA, Affinity Groups and Parent Advisory
meetings helped us identify communication gaps, particularly in areas like
homework policies and parent support strategies. Through these
partnerships, we were able to communicate school priorities and gather
feedback that informed how we are shaping future communications with
families.

e Instagram and Social Media Outreach: To better connect with families and
promote the learning happening at IMS, we created a school Instagram account.
This allowed us to visually showcase student projects, classroom activities, and
school events in a way that reached parents who were active on social media.

o For example: Posts on Instagram highlighted learning experiences from
classrooms, such as student-led projects or interactive lessons, giving families
a window into daily student life. While we saw an increase in engagement
through this platform, feedback suggested that many parents preferred
Facebook over Instagram, leading us to consider expanding to a more widely
used platform in the future.

e Identifying the Need for Monthly Newsletters: We provide weekly parent
communication that includes important events, updates, and information about
what is happening at IMS. The weekly communication builds on itself is always
available to our families. However, one of the key takeaways from our reflection on
the 2023-2024 communication strategies was the need for even more regular,
structured communication with families that builds their understanding around the
rigorous and engaging curriculum/instruction our students experience on a regular
basis.

o For example: In the 2024-2025 SIP, we have woven monthly newsletters
into our communication plan to provide more content specific information.
These newsletters will feature specific information on what students are
learning in class, and how families can connect and get involved with their
children around that content.


Our Effect:

EES Staff Data: "Staff share a high sense of urgency around the need to improve"
increased from 55% to 76%.

Reflection: While partnerships with the PTSA and Parent Advisory Committee
proved to be valuable avenues for engagement, there were still gaps in our
communication regarding homework and academic expectations. The creation of an
Instagram account helped us showcase learning, but feedback indicated a potential
shift toward Facebook for greater parent engagement. Moving into 2024-2025, we
are addressing these communication challenges by implementing monthly
newsletters and working to improve clarity around rigor and high academic
expectations.

Data That Didn’t Grow as Expected

Respect and Problem-Solving Among Students: Despite efforts, peer-to-peer
respect showed only marginal growth, and there was a slight decline in students
feeling involved in problem-solving.
o Data: Respect from peers grew from 47.9% to 48%, and involvement in
problem-solving decreased from 71% to 69%.
Engagement and Enjoyment of School: Student enjoyment of school did not grow
as hoped, and student engagement with challenging activities remained a concern.
o Data: Student enjoyment of school decreased from 57% to 55%.
Family Communication: Satisfaction with communication around homework and
progress monitoring showed only slight growth, remaining a challenge for many
families.
o Data: Satisfaction with communication about homework increased
marginally from 27.9% to 29%, and opportunities to discuss student
progress improved from 39% to 40%.

How will the results of the 23/24 SIP affect the development of the 24/25 SIP?

Goal 1: Enhancing Reading Skills

What did the data from 2023-2024 reveal:


In the 2023-2024 school year, iReady and SBAC data revealed significant gaps in students’
reading comprehension, particularly in informational text. Although progress was made
with some students, others struggled to achieve expected growth, signaling a need for more
targeted reading interventions.

e Inconsistent Reading Growth: There was a wide disparity in growth, with some
students making significant progress while others remained behind.

o Data: 50.3% of students made at least one year's growth in reading
comprehension, but only 42.1% of 7th graders reached mid-grade level
proficiency.

e Uneven Growth Across Grades: Growth was uneven across grade levels, suggesting
the need for a consistent instructional approach in reading.

o Data: 54.5% of students reached mid or above grade level in informational
text comprehension, with stronger results in 6th grade compared to 7th
grade.

How does this inform the 2024-2025 goal?

This year’s goal is to improve reading comprehension, with a focus on informational text.
Based on last year’s gaps, we will use iReady data to tailor interventions and regularly
track growth, ensuring personalized support for students who are struggling, as well as
those who need enrichment.

e Personalized Interventions: iReady diagnostics will help inform differentiated
instruction, ensuring that interventions are tailored to students’ individual needs.

e Student Engagement: We will implement self-reflection journals to foster student
ownership of reading progress, helping students set personal goals and track their
improvement.

Goal 2: Challenging and Inclusive Education
What did the data from 2023-2024 reveal?

Although we made progress in promoting academic growth, data showed concerns about
student engagement and the need for greater rigor in instruction. Some students felt they
were not sufficiently challenged, and differentiation was not consistent across classrooms.

e Engagement and Challenge: Students reported that they were not consistently
challenged in their learning, leading to concerns about the rigor of instruction.


o Data: Only 72% of students reported that their teachers challenged them to
learn, which represented a slight decline from previous years.

e Effective Use of Data: Staff effectively used data to support struggling students, but
differentiation for higher-performing students needed improvement.

o Data: 89% of staff reported using assessment data to identify students
needing interventions, but only 47% of families felt their children were
challenged academically.

e Inconsistent Enforcement of Expectations: Behavior expectations were not
consistently enforced across classrooms, leading to confusion among students about
what was expected.

= Data: Only 66% of staff felt behavior expectations were consistently
enforced, up from 58% at the start of the year.

How does this inform the 2024-2025 goal?

This year, the goal is to ensure that all students experience rigorous, differentiated
instruction while receiving appropriate supports. We will focus on ensuring that teachers
challenge high-performing students while continuing to support those who struggle.

e Differentiated Instruction: Through PLCs, teachers will collaborate on strategies
to increase instructional rigor and ensure differentiation for all students.

e MTSS and Behavior Intervention: Our MTSS teams will continue to use Behavior
Intervention Forms to track and address behavioral issues, ensuring that students
receive timely support.

e PTSA and Parent Advisory Involvement: We will work with the PTSA and Parent
Advisory Committee to gather feedback from parents and ensure that academic
expectations are communicated clearly.

Goal 3: Cultivating Academic Behaviors
What did the data from 2023-2024 reveal?

Our focus on social-emotional learning (SEL) helped improve student-teacher
relationships, but students struggled with core academic behaviors such as self-advocacy
and time management. Teachers reported progress in SEL practices but felt there was
room for more consistency in reinforcing academic behaviors across subjects.


e Student Ownership: Students lacked self-advocacy and organizational skills, which
impacted their ability to take ownership of their learning.
o Data: 66% of teachers saw growth in academic behaviors like self-advocacy,
but improvement was inconsistent across subjects.
e SELand Academic Behaviors: While SEL practices were embedded in classrooms,
they were not always linked directly to academic skills.
o Data: 66% of staff reported that they integrated SEL into daily lessons, but
more focus on connecting these skills to academic behaviors was needed.

How does this inform the 2024-2025 goal?

This year, we aim to reinforce essential academic behaviors such as self-advocacy,
goal-setting, and time management. Teachers will receive additional support to embed
these behaviors into daily instruction and ensure that students are consistently developing
these skills.

e Reimagining Student Engagement (Amy Berry) Student-Engagement Continuum:
Teachers will use rubrics to assess student growth in academic behaviors, offering
clear feedback on areas such as time management and organization.

e Knowand recognize the academic behaviors that make up the continuum. Reflect on
how they are showing at various points in time, and that they can identify examples
from the continuum that are demonstrated in their evidence.

e Students will not be expected to “drive” all the time, but recognize that there are
active roles to be played in learning.

e Self-Reported Grade - High yield strategy identified by meta-analysis (Hattie) Our
students will become expert learners which requires students to know and use the
continuum to reflect on their engagement levels and set goals.
