A. Executive Administration - 2024 Legislative Session Update; Directors David Berg and Shannon Burch, Legislative Representatives (00:29:18)
Director Berg reminded everyone the legislative session ends March 7 and the budgets are expected to be finalized as early as tomorrow. He reviewed the budget process. A final version will be presented for a single up or down vote without any last amendments. He summarized progress on several bills:
  House Bill 2494 is the MSOC bill to deal with the increases districts have had to absorb for materials, supplies, and operating costs. This includes insurance costs, utilities costs, etc. They are providing less funding now than originally proposed, but the funding will be applied retroactively for the 23-24 school year, so that is a good.

Second substitute Senate Bill 5882 is regarding the paraeducator and office staff funding formula. It has been narrowed down to focus on paras and office staff funding. It has now passed both chambers. The intent of this additional funding is for districts to hire additional support staff, or provide better wages for those already hired in the 2024-25 school year. The superintendent must also collect data showing how the funds were used. It sounds very promising, but the actual increases we expect to see in the bill are for a prototypical elementary school of 400 students. We are currently funded only .936 of a para, with the increase in funding of about 0.11, bringing it up to 1.054 paras for a school of 400 students. The increase for secondary schools is roughly the same, and office support instructional aides, funding went from 2.012 to 2.13, so while we are grateful for the increase, that is 1/10 of a person for a school of 400 students. When we look at the intent of the bill and then see it is only fractions of a person, our expectations were probably not met.

He briefly discussed the substitute HB 1248 transportation bill, to address some of the shortfalls in transportation we are having to make up with our local levy funds, ultimately it doesn't provide a lot for Puyallup because we don't really provide that many private transportation options.

HB 1280 is Special Education Funding cap adjustment. The cap is currently about 13%, which is going to be increased to 16%. It doesn't necessarily affect Puyallup but does affect some of our neighbors. In PSD we currently serve less than the cap, so it doesn't affect us. There is also a study to determine if districts are "overidentifying students" to gain more funding.
  He does look forward to continuing to work with our legislators as we move forward. 

Director Burch added the para bill is also retroactive for 2023-24 as well.

President Romero made several remarks about unfunded mandates, those are things that come out of our local levy dollars, even though our state should be providing funds for these services in the district. He is disappointed that the state thinks we are overassessing special education services for students. He asked the community to help communicate with our legislators.

  C. Career and Technical Education – First Reading CTE Curriculum and New Course Updates; Teri Harris, Director of Career and Technical Education (01:05:25)
Director Teri Harris presented a new course update and curriculum adoption recommendation. She began by saying CTE is working with Walker High School to create a 7th and 8th-grade extension program, AeroExplorers, to be piloted at the Kalles Junior High School campus. She described the details of the proposed program and then presented a summary of each of the other offerings, which were Agroecology, Aerospace Engineering, Tech Foundations, Technical Math.

Our CTE Department is also bringing an update on two new courses to be offered in 2024-2025 school year which are based on OSPI Framework Based Curriculum. Our course design team consisted of our Farm-to-Fork TOSA (Javi Silva), CTE Science and Natural Resources Teacher (Ben Dotson), District CTE TOSA (Stephanie Giustino), CTE General Advisory Chair (Dave Robbs), CTE Assistant Director (Chris Sieg), and CTE Director (Teri Harris).

CTE is developing updated frameworks for Hydroponic Farming for Chemistry credit. This is a year-long OSPI framework-based curriculum in the Science Natural Resources Pathway at Emerald Ridge High School. We are also submitting a new framework for Food Truck Basics for CTE elective credit. This is a semester-long OSPI framework-based curriculum in the Family and Consumer Science – Human Services Pathway at Puyallup High School.

Our CTE Department is bringing a curriculum adoption recommendation for an update to the existing Sports Medicine course offered at the three comprehensive high schools. The adoption team consisted of Sports Medicine teachers from Puyallup High School (Katie Akeson), Emerald Ridge High School (John Morrison), and Rogers High School (Willie London); Puyallup School District CTE TOSA (Stephanie Giustino); CTE General Advisory Chair (Dave Robbs); and former CTE Director of Instructional Leadership (Maija Delaquin).

The board received a curriculum adoption recommendation packet in BoardDocs that includes the adoption rationale for the Cengage Sports Medicine Essentials, 3rd Edition curriculum. The cost of the recommended curriculum would be $282/student bundle which would include a hardcover classroom textbook copy, online subscription, and student workbooks. Purchasing one class set per high school would have a total district cost of $25,500. While it was our preferred option based on content and support, it ranged upwards to $10,500 less expensive than other comparative options.

In terms of our bias screen, for technical content, this was an option that contained little image representation beyond internal body systems. It did offer a balanced representation of race, ethnicity, and gender diversity in the photos that were included. While persons with disabilities, gender identity, family structures, and socio-economic status is not well represented throughout the text, these topics will be addressed through direct instruction, classroom discussion, and videos provided in the lessons to highlight career-relevant factors related to inclusion.

Professional Learning for our instructors will include extensive support from the publisher and authors which include instructor materials, simulated/immersive lab outlines, and collaboration through job-alike and extended time. During the official Community Materials Review that took place May 11 through May 25, 2023, we received no request for additional information and no concerns. Comments from 99 individuals were received during the teacher, student, and parent/guardian preview. Their feedback resulted in the selection of the Cengage Sports Medicine Essentials, 3rd Edition curriculum due to engagement, ease of navigation, accessibility, and career representation.

The process followed in the development of this curriculum adoption recommendation was brought to the Instructional Materials Committee at the October 19, 2023, committee meeting and we received approval that we followed the adoption recommendation process according to Policy 2020 and Regulation 2020R. We welcome any questions you may have regarding this recommendation and will return for Board consideration at the next meeting on March 18th.

Michael Stella asked what the new curriculum addresses that the current curriculum is missing? Director Harris replied the instructor just wanted an updated textbook and we are not expecting any changes in the framework of the class; it will enhance your studies. There were no other questions.
  D. Business Services - January 2024 Financial Statements; Matt Young, Interim Executive Director of Business Services (01:14:41)
Director Young presented the following key messages:
As of January, we are 42% of the way through the fiscal year.  Revenues are at 39.29% and Expenditures are at 40.28% of the operating budget.
We received $3.7 million in state match funds related to the Ferrucci JH expansion.  We estimate there is just under $2.0 million left to be claimed in state match funds on the junior high expansion and newly built elementary school projects.

FUND BALANCE TREND
In January OSPI begins to pay apportionment funding based on actual enrollment rather than budgeted enrollment so you’ll notice the decline in our fund balance trend. This is expected.
  STUDENT ENROLLMENT
Enrollment is holding steady with a small increase from January to February. Most of the increase occurred at the elementary level, which is typical for the start of second semester.

24-25 BUDGET UPDATE: ENROLLMENT PROJECTIONS
Enrollment is the primary driver of our state funding model. The enrollment projections are used for budget development to estimate state basic education funding.
Many different factors are considered:
Birth rates
Cohort survival rates
City/county building permits
Inter- and Intra-district transfers/waivers

Projections are created for each school and grade level. Those projections are used to allocate basic ed classroom staffing to each elementary and secondary school.
In the Fall of 2023, enrollment was down 450 from projection. While we’ve gained enrollment throughout the year, we are still 392 below projection, resulting in $4.2 less in revenue.

Staffing Implications
Need to right-size our staffing based on lower enrollment and flat projection.
We are currently over-staffed according to our class size targets, and some schools will have less basic ed teachers than the current year.
Initial staffing for 24-25 has been distributed to principals (week of 2/26)
There are some elementary classes with some class sizes we are monitoring and may adjust staffing as needed toward end of summer/fall.

There were no questions at this time. President Romero explained what we see in May will directly affect what we see in August at our two budget public hearings with public comment and he encouraged the community to make comments.

Director Names said this year was a big surprise being off by 450 students, for a deficit of $4.2 million. She asked what are we doing now to make more accurate projections for next year? Director Young replied we have dug into our waiver data to show what that trend is showing and how certain areas are impacted by that. We are also looking at our program offerings to adjust the trend for post-COVID trends that haven't yet readjusted. We reduced the projection by nearly half for last year and still over-projected, most notably at our kindergarten level, which was common across the state. As we look at that flat projection, we reduced to that level based on that.
