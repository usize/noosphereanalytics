  D. Superintendent's Report; Dr. John Polm, Superintendent (00:04:20)
Dr Polm welcomed our new Student Representative Cimerra Killian Porter from Rogers HS.

Superintendent Report - Dr. Polm spoke on how decisions are made on weather-related delays and cancellations, the winter sports season, recognition of musicians and athletes at the school level, the AASA Conference he attended, and he noted that later on the agenda he will be presenting the district's newly drafted strategic plan. 
  He discussed the Gender-Inclusive Schools policy and regulation, 3211 and 3211R and explained the governing statute that requires the policy and regulation is RCW 28A.600.477 and RCW 28A.642.80, which is referred to as the Transgender Student Policy and Procedure. The policy and regulation have been in place since 2014 with revisions in 2020 and 2022. The laws and policies related to gender-inclusive schools in intended to protect some of our most vulnerable students.

E. Student Representative's Report; Cimerra Killian Porter, Rogers High School (00:48:57)
Cimerra introduced herself and said she is one of the eight student representatives for the district. She is a senior at Rogers and part of Army JROTC. She shared some of the results of her seven-question survey where she spoke with 22 random students, and due to time constraints, will share the remaining info at the next board meeting. She said the majority of students stated there is a lack of communication on opportunities in the district, and seniors feel that specifically about graduation requirements. High school students would like to hear more about next steps after graduation and what is offered outside of high school. Students would like more inclusion of diversity within classrooms and hallways. Most have a teacher they can go to if need be, especially if this teacher is also a coach or part of an outside school activity. When asked if they feel safe at school, the majority replied they do; however, students in the LGBTQ+ community feel concealed in the hallways and sometimes in classrooms. When communicating with teachers, some students feel that teachers can be understanding if they know what is going on but others don't like the excuse they are being given.

  B. Equity and Instructional Leadership - Multilingual Learner Program Updates: Jamie Lee, Director of Categorical Federal Programs, Title/LAP/MLL/DL (02:43:57)
Director Jamie Lee presented the multilingual education available in the Puyallup School District. Before she began, she offered the option of listening to this presentation in Spanish by wearing this device. Ceci Tamayo, the Title/LAP/ML program instructional coach, provided the Spanish interpretation of tonight’s presentation.

As she transitioned to overseeing this department in the last couple of months, she has been able to meet with each staff to revise and renew our commitment to the linguistically diverse students in our district. Our program goal is to see our students’ strengths and support what they need. We know that all students can learn if we teach them intentionally and explicitly. We are responsible for student success. And finally, it takes quality curriculum, solid instruction, and aligned assessment to impact student learning.
As of February 3rd, there are 1899 students identified as multilingual learners in PSD. Families complete the state-required Home Language Survey during registration which triggers our department to screen the students. All multilingual students have to be assessed in WIDA, which is a test used in 41 states. There are 4 components to WIDA – Reading, Speaking, Listening, and Writing. Each component takes about 60 minutes. Students are tested in small groups, about 5-6 students, Kindergarten is 1:1. WIDA assessment takes about 1-2 months.

There are 6 elementary certificated teachers and 17 paras. At the secondary level, there are 8 certificated teachers and 6 paras. A total of 37 staff are serving 1900 students, which is about 51 students per staff. Based on our district-implemented benchmark assessment, multilingual students in our district are making growth academically in reading. This growth can also be seen in our dual language classes at Karshner and Dessie Evans. Students are reading at grade level in Spanish and achieving as their gen ed peers in English. Even with the two years of remote learning, we are still on the trajectory of growth as students reach their 4th and 5th grades. More importantly than their academic growth, students are learning to embrace what it means to be multilingual and culturally diverse within the school setting.

Moving forward, they are working to maximize funding to lower the current 51 students to 1 teacher ratio. They have already started to work together as a department to align our practices and service models to strengthen what students experience as their core instruction. There will be a grant opportunity to cover fees and tuition for teachers to get their ML endorsement and they have plans to apply and receive this additional funding. We are shifting mindsets that students who speak another language are a strength, not a limitation.

There was a short Q&A with discussion and comments from board members following her presentation.
  C. Operations - Bell Time Task Force Update; Dr. Vincent Pecchia, Assistant Superintendent of Operations and School Support (02:25:09)
In alignment with one of the superintendent’s goals, review of bell schedule and transportation efficiencies via task force. The Bell Time Task Force Charter outlines the following deliverables:
Per Policy 6600, Transportation, create and recommend new efficient bus routes that improve on-time performance starting with the 2023-2024 school year.
Incorporate recent Safety Advisory Committee recommendations for safe walking routes.
Assess future needs/facilities master planning to ensure students have equitable access to programs.
Present to stakeholder groups, district staff and/or the board of directors, as needed.

As a reminder, the task force membership includes a consultant, staff, students, and parents. The taskforce had their first meeting on September 15 with monthly meetings held thereafter except for December. Stakeholder engagement kicked off in January with virtual informational events and the first of two surveys. An in-depth discussion of bell-time scenarios and survey results is planned in March with a recommendation for the board to consider taking action on a recommendation in April. Pending the board’s consideration to take action, new bell-times could start as early as September 2023.

Task force members engaged in an in-depth analysis and discussion of each region’s start and end times, traffic patterns, and other unique community considerations such as growth and walking routes. The task force members applied their learning of each region to several tiered bell time model scenarios using the guiding questions displayed on this presentation slide. He added as a reminder, the bell time scenarios are conceptual in nature and meant to spark discussion amongst task force members and stakeholders.

Scenario one, elementary schools are divided into three groups, junior high school is first along with the first elementary group. The fleet is reutilized for high school and the second elementary group before the final elementary group is served. This scenario is plausible and closely aligns with our current bell time model. This model will require additional staffing to balance student loads and increase on-time performance.

Scenario two uses four tiers and requires streamlining the start of school times across the district. Junior high schools are serviced first. The fleet is then reutilized for high school before elementary group one and two are serviced. This scenario is plausible given current staffing levels and allows a greater reutilization of school buses to complete multiple runs within each morning and afternoon route. The goal is to use each school bus at least three times every morning and afternoon to increase efficiencies.

Scenario three takes adolescent brain research into account and is favored by students on our task force. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention share that “Adolescents become sleepy later at night and need to sleep later in the morning as a result in shifts in biological rhythms. These biological changes are often combined with poor sleep habits. During the school week, school start times are the main reason students wake up when they do. The combination of late bedtimes and early school start times results in most adolescents not getting enough sleep.” So, in this scenario, an early elementary group is serviced first, then a late start elementary group and high school, and lastly junior high school. This scenario is plausible given current staffing levels and allows for a greater reutilization of school buses within each morning and afternoon route. There are some challenges to address such as junior high school students participating in high school advanced classes and athletics.

Scenario four is a common model used by school districts. Junior high schools are serviced first, then high school along with an early start elementary group. A late-start elementary group is serviced last. This scenario is NOT plausible given current staffing levels because run times interfere with each other. We would have to hire up to fifty more bus drivers before the start of the 2023-2024 school year to make this scenario work and that’s not a realistic goal given the nationwide bus driver shortage and its impact at the local level.

The district’s communication department launched the first of two surveys last January. The survey focused on current bus rider experience and bell schedule. The survey was opened for two weeks with 3,373 respondents. Survey results that include quantitative and qualitative data will be shared with the board during the March 10th study session.

  D. Executive Administration - Strategic Plan Presentation; Dr. John Polm, Superintendent and Stephanie Watson, Senior Director, District Management Group (02:59:46)
Dr. Polm presented the draft strategic plan for your review this evening. This plan, once approved, will provide the framework for our initiative inventory and prioritization, the establishment of specific metrics, action plans and steps for each of our initiatives. He introduced Stephanie Watson, who is joining us via Zoom from the East Coast.
  Dr. Polm reminded that our goal was to update the strategic priorities the board had set in 2011. It had been over a decade since a process of reviewing the priorities, and a lot has changed. We engaged with District Management Group to assist in the process. We started the process last summer and commenced with a needs analysis through the fall of 2022. The Steering Committee engaged in regular meetings with some outside work, and a draft was completed in January 2023.

This slide represents the framework of the strategic planning and notes the stages that are part of the design portion. After the plan is adopted, DMG hands the process off to the district and we will then refine any measurable goals along with an inventory of our initiatives. Finally, we will develop action steps related to the various initiatives. Once the draft was completed in January, we received and incorporated more feedback. Part of this included the study session with the school board on January 20th. He noted that the plan has a next phase called “implementation”. We still have quite a bit of work to do as we work on yearly targets for each measure, inventory our current initiatives and index those to the priorities, perform a gap analysis, and finalize a new set of initiatives.

Finally, we will create action steps for the initiatives and set up metrics and steps to monitor progress over the next five years. The final plan becomes embedded into our work and helps us align our priorities with resources, time, programs, and activities. He switched from the PPT to the draft strategic plan itself and explained each page.

He asked if there any questions or discussion for Stephanie Watson or him? There were several comments by the board. They appreciate that their previous comments were incorporated and appreciate the time the team has dedicated to this process. Prioritization was derailed due to the pandemic.
