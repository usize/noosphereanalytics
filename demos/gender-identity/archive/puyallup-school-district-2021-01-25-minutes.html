  2. Superintendent's Report
A. Superintendent's Report; Dr. John Polm, Superintendent
Open Public Meetings Act (OPMA) - Dr. Polm said some people have questioned why the school board meetings are not held in-person now that we are back to school (for some). So people are aware, he said the latest information is that the board may not hold in-person public meetings pursuant to RCW 43.06.210. In-person meetings are not permitted until the end of the declared emergency or further action by the Legislature or Governor. Consequently, we will continue with our online meetings for the time being. We hope that everyone has access and can view, listen and in some cases, participate.

Hybrid Return to In-Person Instruction - Comments on Hybrid Planning – there is no report tonight. We are continuing to work on the plan and will have presentation on Feb 1. But at this time, he would like to remind the board and those listening that our staff have been working collaboratively with our bargaining groups on ways to work within the health and safety guidance to bring students in for in-person instruction. There have been rumors characterizing this process as adversarial, but this is not the case. He thanked district leadership and the leadership in our labor groups who have been solution-oriented, patient, and focused on the appropriate health and safety protocols needed to be in place to return students and staff to school sites. It is also important to note that as the 8th Largest SD in the state, Puyallup is one of the few districts in Western Washington to have as many students on campus as we have. This week, we'll be up to about 26% of our students receive in-person on-site instruction this week for at least part of their week; 4,800 are students. He gave credit to our staff and the collaborative nature to all participating.

Dr. Polm said with regard to athletics, we are still on track to start next week as planned. Athletic Directors are continuing to finalize what this means. District AD Jim Meyerhoff will provide more info later when we know more details, and most info will go out through school ADs. The plan for junior high is to offer intramural activities. We don't know if we'll be able to be back in full force later in the year but at this point it looks like intramural. The rumors are not true that we aren't doing anything.
  3. Communications
A. District Highlights
Gillispie said tonight we kick off our highlights this evening celebrating the return of our youngest learners back into our buildings. Families of Kindergarten and First Grade students who selected the in-person hybrid learning model returned to our buildings the week of Jan. 12, two days per week. The K-1 schedule will increase to four days per week tomorrow, Jan. 26, when we return students in grades 2-3, two days per week Two weeks from tomorrow’s date, we will follow with the return of students in grades 4-6 to campus 2 days per week on Feb. 9. Some may be wondering how we are able to return all of our elementary students to campus during high COVID-19 activity levels. In person learning is permissible for students in these grades (in addition to those with the highest needs throughout our district) in small groups of 15 or less per classroom. Our existing class sizes for hybrid allow us the ability to accommodate students onsite with strict health and safety measures.

Adjustments have been made to our school environment to keep everyone safe and well. So most of the first day focused around the teaching and learning of new health and safety protocols. A student’s instructional day actually starts at home before coming to school, when families are required to submit an electronic health screen form before their child boards a bus or is dropped off at school. This is followed with an in-classroom temperature check after entering the classroom. Students were taught about proper mask wearing and what 6-ft spacing looks like to properly social distance. In addition, staff reviewed hand sanitizing expectations for lunch and recess.

As we continue to plan for and celebrate the return of students to campus, there are many resources available on the district website. This includes our Return to School Playbook outlining safety expectations and COVID response protocols, Department of Health information on the recently updated new guidance for schools, and a link to a recent Welcome Back Town Hall event with special guest Dr. Anthony Chen, the Director of Tacoma-Pierce County Health Department.

We realize some may feel uneasy about returning to in-person learning, but a lot of folks around here are just simply overjoyed to see students in-person again. Students are excited, too. To quote a student from Karshner Elementary, "I feel like a superhero coming back to school with a mask on!"

She presented a 2½-minute video giving you just a glimpse into this exciting first day for students.

Next, we are proud to spotlight our Ready for Kindergarten program and staff. For years, the District has offered free Ready for Kindergarten classes for families within the Puyallup School District boundaries. These classes encourage parents to talk, sing, read and play with their child in simple ways that foster essential pre-literacy, pre-math and social-emotional skills. Age-appropriate activities are designed to begin at birth and continue through age five. The curriculum is based on current research and replicates what parents and educators of successful students have done for years. Parents are children’s most powerful teacher. Our Ready for Kindergarten program provides them with the tools to plant the seeds for success in school and a lifelong love of learning.

This year, our Ready for Kindergarten workshops went virtual and three preschool teachers stand out in the crowd for facilitating an engaging first session. Thank you to Jordan Irvan from Stewart Elementary, Karen Duggan from Maplewood Elementary and Ariel Hallberg from Sunrise Elementary for their amazing work in setting up our families for success. Karen and Jordan did a great job leading the orientation, while Ariel helped with planning and putting the kits together. We had nearly 60 families register for this first session. The next workshop session begins March 25.

Ready for Kindergarten workshops are designed to help families and caregivers prepare young children for Kindergarten, before Kindergarten. By working together, we can ensure the best possible start for school and academic success.

MLK Observance and Cultural Awareness in PSD
This month we remember and celebrate civil rights activist, Martin Luther King, Jr. Puyallup School District honors his achievements, making equity and inclusion a top priority the past several years creating environments of respect and learning that inspire students to reach their full potential.

We have an intentional focus on building awareness, increasing knowledge, developing skills, and promoting advocacy for cultural awareness. Racial injustice creates disparities in education and limits our ability to thrive as a healthy community. Puyallup School District has made an intentional effort to:
Hire staff representative of our student population
Provide professional learning focused on equity and social justice
Create ‘safe zone’ spaces for staff and students
In 2016, the District developed a plan that was focused on implementing systemic change. We listened as students and community members shared powerful, authentic, and personal experiences around racial injustice. By uncovering personal biases, staff learned how to have courageous conversations about race with the following agreements: (1) stay engaged, (2) experience discomfort, (3) speak your truth, (4) expect and accept nonclosure, (5) maintain a learning stance & remain open to new things, and (6) refrain from pivoting to other “-isms”. As a result of this learning, this conversation framework is actively used during professional learning throughout the district.

High quality professional learning for staff focused on equity and social justice results in school leaders setting meaningful equity goals with staff which, in turn, helps educate students. A focus on equity and social justice creates a cultural competency that impacts discipline, enrollment in programs, and overall student achievement.

Purposeful and intentional relationship building also helps counter stereotypes. Staff and students are encouraged to form relationships with people who identify differently than them with the goal of countering stereotypes by changing hearts and minds. Administrative Equity Walks were conducted to determine if professional learning for staff focused on equity and social justice was, in fact, transferring to schools and classrooms at large.

Our district has two distinct and comprehensive nondiscrimination policies. Policy 3210, focuses on the equal and educational opportunity and treatment for all students. Policy 3211 specifically recognizes the importance of an inclusive approach to supporting transgender students. We are proud of our policy work focused on nondiscriminatory practices and inclusivity.

As a result of this work, ‘Safe Zones’ were created that represent a safe space for students to retreat to if they are experiencing distress caused from harassment, intimidation and/or bullying. Over 60 employees in our secondary schools have been trained on this support model and consequently proudly display their Safe Zone sign on classroom and office doors.

Racism and inequality affect everyone who are marginalized, particularly our communities of color, and perpetuates division. We continue to work with urgency and intentionality to drive critical changes in our system, deepening our work in culturally responsive practices. Our schools are environments of respect and learning for every child, teaching students to advocate for their right to be treated with kindness and compassion. Our work is ongoing and our goal is that our systems will continue to reflect equity and justice in schools.

A. Communities In Schools Update; Jan Mauk, Executive Director, Communities In Schools Puyallup
Dr. Polm said we have three reports this evening. He introduced Executive Director of Communities In Schools Jan Mauk, who thanked him and the board for this opportunity and said she is very excited to be here tonight to update the board on the work of Communities In Schools in our district.

Communities In Schools of Puyallup is dedicated to empowering students to stay in school and on a path to a brighter future, by Increasing student access to integrated student supports, through evidenced based work, to ensure that all kids have the same opportunity to succeed, through supports such as social emotional, academic development, attendance, trauma informed care, school climate, basic needs and opportunities for community service. We empower students to break through any personal challenges in their lives, so they, can define the future they want. No matter what it looks like, each student’s individual success adds up to a movement of changes, that tear down structural barriers, and builds an equitable path, to education for future generations.

Mauk said since it’s been quite some time since she has been before the board, she began with a story and an update on the work we did last school year. This is a story one of our site coordinators shared with us: Earlier this year, she had learned of a student that was going to be unenrolled from the school due to ongoing, consistent absences during continuous learning. This particular student had been a part of our weekend backpack program in the past and the site coordinator had regular contact with the mom last year, but had struggled to make contact this year. When the site coordinator learned that the student was going to be unenrolled, she spoke with the counselor and principal to see what they could do to support the family and get the student engaged. They agreed to do a home visit to see what barriers they were facing. When they met the mom, they learned that she was very overwhelmed and just needed some extra support. They were able to get the family a hot spot and the site coordinator let the mom know that she could call her if she needed help navigating the online platforms. The next day, the mom called the site coordinator to help her daughter get into the Teams meeting. They got her in and she was very engaged and excited to be a part of class. The site coordinator has been checking in with the student’s teacher to see if she is attending class and the teacher has said that the student is not only attending, but also going above and beyond in her work. Site coordinators act as caring adults, for not only students, but their families, to build transformative relationships and who walk by their side to connect them to community resources designed to help them succeed.

In the 2019-20 school year we had seven full-time CIS Site Coordinators working directly in seven elementary schools to connect kids to caring adults and community resources designed to help them succeed. In partnership with the local community, we do whatever we can to ensure that all kids – regardless of the challenges they may face; have what they need to realize their potential. Through our model of integrated students supports, we served 595 students with ongoing services last school year and 6,891 students received at least one service. Of our case managed students, 75% qualified for Free and reduced Lunch, 13% of them were experiencing homelessness and 55% were black, indigenous or people of color.

Some of our core programs include our Good Samaritan Readers program, where we served 157 students prior to the closure of school in March by pairing them with a trained caring volunteer tutor.

Our Weekend Food Backpack Program, in which we served 375 students, After-school math tutoring for 30 students, attendance club 36 students and 48 of our students were being mentored by a caring adult. Also prior to the school closure over 5,000 students had the opportunity to participate in a community service project through our district-wide March Gladness program. And our Fill the Canoe school supply drive supplied or $40,000 worth of school supplies to all 33 schools in the district.

During the school Closure our Site Coordinators shifted along with everyone else in learning how to serve our students and families remotely. From March to June our site coordinator provided over 2,000 check-ins with students and families. They provided academic assistance to student virtually over 60 times, Weekend food was distributed 1,450 times and over $15,000 in basic needs were donated and distributed to families.

During the summer through partnerships with the community we were able to distribute family meal boxes to families in need. This fall in partnership with Red Canoe Credit Union, and donations for our generous community, we have been able to purchase $11,000 of school supplies for schools throughout the district and have distributed thousands of dollars of donated supplies as well.

In November, with the help of many individual donations that were matched up to $2,000 by Walker Real Estate, and various community partners, through Operation Turkey we were able to serve 701 students with Thanksgiving support. In December, our Holiday help campaign supported 809 students with donations coming from our online Holiday Help Campaign, various community partners including: Motion Church, City of Puyallup Parks and Recreation and Northwest Fitness company.

  C. Business Services - November 2020 Financial Statements; Laura Marcoe, Executive Director of Finance & Accountability
Tonight, Laura Marcoe presented the November financial statements. She said we have added the operating budget to the reports. Some of the big changes we made to the revenues are: Reducing state apportionment revenue by $14M – related to the decrease in enrollment, we've added $9.3 M to local property taxes due to the passage of the supplemental levy, adjusted all state and federal grants to the actual grant awards plus any carryover from the prior year, and reduced some other local revenues like rentals (as we’re not currently renting our facilities) and investment interest (as the interest rates we receive have decreased).

At the point we settled on the operating budget, she was projecting total revenues to be about $8M less overall than the original adopted budget. However, that doesn’t take into account any further stimulus funds we may receive from either the state or federal government. We are expecting a second round of CARES act stimulus funding, but we don’t know yet exactly what that will look like, so it’s not included in the operating budget.

On the expenditure side of the operating budget, we didn’t make a ton of adjustments as we still have a lot of unknowns. We did realize savings in staff costs in some areas due to staff that were temporarily furloughed at the beginning of the year, but we have also had increased staff costs in other areas like our COVID coordinators and temporary recess paras.
Now that we are working to bring more students back to school, we anticipate that some salary items like coaching contracts and additional work stipends will now start to be spent.

Right now it’s extremely difficult to project a year-end fund balance. We just have so many unknowns. The legislature is in session, so we’re watching very closely to see what decisions they might make that would affect this year’s funding. Like she mentioned before, we also have a second stimulus package coming, but we don’t know all the details yet. Right now, the best we can do is continue to remain conservative and model every bit of information we get to see what affect it has on our financial outlook.

She said November is the last month of substantial receipts for fall property tax collections. We received $2.1 M in the General Fund; $2.4 M in the debt service fund and $63,000 in the capital projects fund related to our technology levy. It is important to note this was the last tax collection for the technology levy as 2020 was the final year of that six-year levy.

Enrollment continues to decline for the 4th month in a row, data as of December. The decrease in December of about 100 FTE was split about 50/50 between elementary and high school. This takes our annual average enrollment as of December to approximately 1,350 students below our original projection. We didn't have January's data yet in time for this report, but preliminarily, we have a decline in enrollment again in January. We are watching closely and calculating what those effects are on our financial statements going forward.

President Yang thanked her and agreed it sounds like so many unknowns we won't get a clear picture until the year is over.
