  2. Board Study Session
A. Equity and Instructional Leadership - On the Journey to Recovery; Dr. Vincent Pecchia, Assistant Superintendent of Equity and Instructional Leadership, and Amanda Kraft, Executive Director of Equity and Professional Learning
Dr. Polm thanked everyone for attending and said it may be shorter than two hours today. We will be focusing in on the work that the district has been engaged in over the last year we're calling On the Journey to Recovery, as well as the academic and social-emotional supports that we've put in place as we've been engaging with the return to school from remote learning and the recovery from all the impacts of the pandemic.  He introduced Dr. Vincent Pecchia, Assistant Superintendent of Equity and Instructional Leadership and Amanda Kraft, Executive Director of Equity and Professional Learning.

Dr. Pecchia said this report updates the board with activities related to the district's approved Academic and Student Well-Being Recovery Plan. In 2021 the Office of Superintendent of Public Instruction required that each Washington State School District develop and submit an Academic and Student Well-Being Recovery Plan by June 1, 2021. They had to include recovery supports to be developed and implemented during the 2021 through 2024 school years. The Board of Directors approved our district's plan on May 24th, 2021. In March 2021, the district launched an Academic and Student Well-Being Recovery Plan survey. The survey stated that the district was interested in hearing from staff and families on the best ways to allocate resources. What stood out in the survey data and shared back in 2021 is that a significant number of respondents, over 50% actually, strongly agreed that providing mental health services and supports and addressing learning loss among students is where the district should prioritize their resources. He is joined this morning with Amanda Kraft, Executive Director of Equity and Professional Learning, and Candice McGregor, Instructional Coach, and they will provide the board with a report focused on an update of activities aligned with the district's Academic and Student Well-Being Recovery Plan. These activities are in response to staff and family survey data and support the district's initiative to teach the "whole" child. Included in today's report is an update on professional learning, instructional coaches, Summer Learning Academy, social-emotional learning, teachers on special assignment or TOSAs, and the inaugural peer student summit that occurred last week. He said Executive Director Kraft will start by sharing information about the heart of our Academic and Student Well-Being Recovery Plan and that is professional learning focused on diversity, equity, and inclusion.

Director Kraft said she is excited to have the opportunity to share with you today about the work in Instructional Leadership. We introduced the theme Belonging is Essential last June at our annual June Planning days. For the last five years, we have hosted two days in June for our professional learning facilitators to come together to build skills in culturally responsive teaching, equity, diversity, and inclusion. During this time, we calibrate our facilitation methods and plan for courses that will take place the following year, building collective efficacy. The theme Belonging is Essential was determined based on the community feedback and our initiative to teach the whole child. Krownapple and Cobb, the authors of Belonging Through a Culture of Dignity The Key to Successful Equity Implementation, defines belonging as the extent to which people feel appreciated, validated, accepted, and treated fairly within an environment. When students feel that they belong, they aren't worried and distracted about being treated as a stereotype or a thin slice of their multidimensional identity. Instead, they are confident that they are seen as human beings, a person of value. This year our facilitators have woven the theme of belonging into over 100 courses. We challenged our teacher leaders to be willing to use innovative strategies to disrupt the status quo and make the learning experience accessible and rich for all youth. Our goal is to improve academic outcomes caused by the pandemic through innovative strategies.

In August our administrative staff comes together for a three-day conference focused on equity, social justice, and inclusion. Our focus has been on building skills, knowledge, and awareness to create an equitable system where all staff and students feel a sense of belonging. The years leading up to where we are now were focused on understanding identity, privilege, and bias coupled with listening to our students' and staff's experiences. Using a Sense of Belong as our theme, we moved into the advocacy stage of the culturally responsive framework. This year we are using the work from Krownapple and Cobb to paint the way. Krownapple and Cobb state that a shared vision is essential for a group of people to change from "who they are" to "who they want to be"; it offers the possibility of a common identity that requires people to first find their starting points by looking inward to assess their current identities, values, and beliefs. From that existing state, the vision provides clarity of purpose and direction. Our purpose is for every student to feel they belong in our schools. To do this, we must listen to students. Each of our schools created a student Equity Advisory Committee that met regularly to share experiences, offer guidance, and provide insight into the student experience. Many of these students were also chosen to participate in our Peer Summit, which you will hear more about later on in the presentation. As defined earlier, a sense of belonging means that students aren't worried and distracted about being treated as a stereotype or a thin slice of their multidimensional identity. It means they feel safe. To ensure that students have a safe space with a trained adult we relaunched Safe Zone. Safe Zone provides identifiable access for students needing immediate help and support from situations caused by social and emotional distress. Safe Zone supports students who feel intimidated, threatened, alone, nervous, sad, controlled, confused, worried, and concerned for their safety! A young person can ask for help at any of the identified Safe Zoned locations in each of our junior high or high schools. In March, just recently, we launched our first elementary Safe Zone class and we now have 60 elementary staff members trained and ready to support students. Teaching and learning is the business we are in, so it should be no surprise that we had a laser-like focus on the teaching practices used daily in classes that would foster a sense of belonging. We have six universal high leverage practices infused into our "look fors" when observing teachers. These practices are infused into every professional learning opportunity. Principals have spent time calibrating with their colleagues to ensure that we are looking for evidence that fosters a culture for learning and that students are engaged in meaningful learning experiences.

One of our challenges has been finding a way to measure the implementation of our equitable practices. With the launch of Panorama, we now have data that highlight our strengths and provide direction for improvement areas. The data from Panorama will be reflected in each school improvement plan (SIP) as they are sometimes references. During our administrative professional learning, we have spent time collectively discussing students' daily experiences and the correlation that has to each of our mission and vision statements. Within the school improvement plan, we are to write an equity statement that creates agency, ensuring the use of innovative strategies to make the learning experience accessible and rich for all youth.

She displayed a wordle created during our administrative professional learning. After discussion, shared reading, and shared learning, teams reflected on what we believe every student must know, understand, and be able to do. From here, we revisited and revised our mission statements and our narratives of the student experience within each school to reflect the values we collectively said were important.

Looking ahead, we will be using our school improvement plans as living documents that tell our story of creating a sense of belonging. Students must have physiological and safety addressed before achievement and self-actualization can occur. By focusing on student wellness through mental health, we minimize the belonging gap and increase the intellectual capacity of our students. Krownapple and Cobb remind us that for students from historically excluded and marginalized groups, the consequences of belonging uncertainty have a disproportionately intense impact. Our focus on equity, diversity, and inclusion through instructional methods, social-emotional learning, and safe spaces will be a variable in creating a sense of belonging.

Next year, our theme is We Belong. Building off belonging is essential and we will focus on our purpose of creating inclusive schools where belonging is essential. We will create agency through collective efficacy and our Panorama data. She asked what questions might the board have about professional learning in particular?

Director Kraft continued that they are excited that our Academic and Student Well-Being Recovery Plan included the implementation of K-12 Instructional Coaches to support our teachers. A coach's primary purpose is to ensure Equity through quality first instruction by providing direct support to teachers through mentoring, curriculum implementation, professional learning, and planning instruction. Our coaches work with our teachers across our K-12 system coupled with an area of emphasis, providing direct support to directors and teachers in specific content areas. Two coaches serve in each content and one coach in the following areas, mentoring, Pre AP and AP, EL and GLAD support, and CTE.

Our coaches use an assets-based approach. Assets can be found in themselves as well as their environment. We're looking for possibility and potential everywhere. When we focus on strengths, we can access the positive emotions that inspire action and open us to learning. Asset-based coaching strives to magnify strengths, and what we focus on grows. In partnership with our clients (our teachers), we seek out what they're doing that's effective, we highlight their working practices and bring to light their hidden skills.

Our mission is that we believe equitable, asset-based, responsive coaching will result in teachers and students reaching their highest potential. Our vision is to Inspire. Empower. Impact. Next, Director Kraft introduced Candice McGregor one of our instructional coaches with an emphasis in mentoring and school support. She is going to share the details about our program, some of the goals, and highlights from this year.

Candice McGregor explained their coaching program is serving over 200 teachers in their 1st, 2nd, and 3rd of teaching. We are all serving all teachers in core content areas. The addition of coaches this year has been overwhelmingly successful. We've been able to change not only who we mentor, but how we mentor. The collective capacity of the group has helped us grow in knowledge and skills. Each coach on the team offers their area of expertise as they reflect and learn from each other. With the increased team, our new teachers are getting more focused support from dedicated coaches because we are working with fewer mentees. Last year she was serving 26 first-year teachers, so with 2nd and 3rd-year teachers added, she had a total of 57 mentees on my roster. It was difficult to provide additional support where needed due to the number of people I was working with. She is happy to report that this year our coaches are serving an average of 7 first-year teachers, and we're able to use the coaching cycles in a much more intentional and impactful way. We are more responsive and collaborative in the support we provide.

In addition to serving new teachers, we are also building the capacity of experienced teachers through our partnership with directors and administrators. Our team works with administrators throughout the year to gauge what support is needed and align our work with building improvement plans and district goals. Coaches work with directors within their emphasis areas to design professional learning, facilitate PLCs, and partner in curriculum support, including adoptions and implementation. It has been transformational having a dedicated team to focus on this work. In previous years their smaller team was able to respond to needs as we could, but with a larger team we have built-in systems to support growth and movement.

McGregor shared that Job Alike mentors are provided to teachers who are new to the profession, new to the district, or new to their position. Their team works with administrators to match these teachers up with mentors who work in their same building and same position as much as possible. Mentors provide day-to-day on-the-job support. She displayed a graph that shows how mentees recently rated the support being provided. The highest types of support reported were emotional support, institutional support, and support with curriculum and materials, which closely align the goals of the program. She played a video showing a few clips of some mentees sharing their experience with job alike support this year.

McGregor said the last three entries on this graph show the number of mentees who were able to participate in classroom visits and observations. As a part of the job alike support program, novice teachers are allotted 7.5 hours of shared release time with their mentors to visit each other's classrooms or observe a mentor teacher together. These numbers may look low, but they are happy to report that they were able to provide this support to as many mentees as possible. Classroom visits are a valuable practice and were very tough to execute the past few years with virtual teaching, health and safety protocols, and a substitute shortage. Most classroom visits this year were made possible by our coach team working creatively to provide coverage in classrooms so that substitutes would not be required.

The emotional support provided is a celebration for this team, as well as how many mentees reported that they felt their mentor provided a safe space for them to share and to reflect and grow. They attribute this success to the resources available to our job alike support mentors, which include inviting them to attend OSPI Mentor Academies, district round tables where they can collaborate on mentoring skills, and a monthly publication written by our team to share mentor tips and timely topics to cover with teachers. She presented some next steps for job alike support. We just held our final round table of the year, and mentors were also asked to reflect on their experience – we will use their feedback, as well as the feedback gathered from mentees, to make adjustments or additions to enhance the mentorship program. We will continue meeting with our stakeholder team, which consists of administrators, coaches, mentors, directors, and new teachers. We use this team to align how to assign mentors to support teachers, communicate expectations, and triangulate the support between coach, job alike mentor, and administrators. Last, we will begin again in August with a kickoff event for mentors before school begins where we share expectations and resources, set goals, and check in with mentors as they begin to build relationships with their teachers. We're looking forward to continuing to provide this support next year.
