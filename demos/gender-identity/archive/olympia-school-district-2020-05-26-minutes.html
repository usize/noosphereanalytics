3.1 Competency Based Credit Policies; 1st Reading
OSD Board Vice President Scott Clifthorne and Mick Hart, Executive Director of Secondary Education presented the 1st reading of Competency Based Credit Policies. 
  Competency-Based Learning (CBC) encompasses strategies that should be broadly engaged throughout teaching and learning, “competency-based crediting” is a specific crediting opportunity. Competency-based crediting is an option for students to earn high school credit for performance on district-specified assessments that are clearly aligned to learning standards. In addition, students may be awarded credit for a variety of competency-based learning that takes place largely outside of traditional classroom instruction. These could include Alternative Learning Experience (ALE), Work-based Learning, and equivalency courses of study.  Washington State has the goal that all students who graduate from high school will be career- and college-ready and that they will be ready for whatever post-secondary pathway they wish to pursue.

  3.3 2020-2021 Budget Presentation
Assistant Superintendent Jennifer Priddy began her presentation noting that the reduction proposals total $4.3 million, a sizable change from what we initially expected. In March, the district expecting a deficit to be at $5.4 million. 
  A summary of reduction options was presented:
Central Administration ($255,000 in Reductions)
Custodial, Grounds, Maintenance and Transportation ($366,000 in Reductions)
Districtwide Finances ($583,000 in Reductions
Classroom Teacher Staffing Allocations to Schools ($2 million in Reductions; $224,000 in Enhancement)
Administrative/School Supports ($656,000 in Reductions)
Assistant Superintendent Priddy continued to present the district wide issues which vary in the proposal. The big factor is that the district will identify where it can push costs on to other fund sources, including categorical programs and transferring the costs of some general fund payroll to programs where a staff member is supporting that program. The district is also identifying small ways that it can reduce some of its ergonomic purchases, travel, and the delay in curriculum adoption.  The reduction in the curriculum adoption budget to $150,000 instead of $450,000 contributed to the budget reduction.
  Classroom Teacher Staffing Allocations to Schools show a reduction of $2 million, but it also includes an enhancement of $224,000. There is a reduction of eleven (11) secondary teachers which is a combination of changes for enrollment where a school is either increasing enrollment or seeing a diminishing of their enrollment and staffing allocations would change based on those types of workload drivers.  It also includes an increase of class size.  Another reduction of 5.1 of elementary schoolteachers based on enrollment is noted in the summary.  There could be no overall change in elementary enrollment, but there is a shift between 3rd and 4th grade changing classroom teachers. Both represent reductions that are within the attrition that the district has experienced.  Also noted is the increase in elementary teachers necessary to expand the Art, Music, and P.E. Program for the next three (3) schools with the next highest level of poverty.  The extension would cover a total of six (6) district schools for the 20-21SY.  
  Assistant Superintendent Priddy noted a glitch in the summary of reduction option document noting that the paraeducator component and the teacher staffing component are combined.  She further explained the correction as it is not another $145,000 reduction to the Art, Music, and PE program. It's an expansion of the Art, Music, and P.E. Program
  Superintendent Murphy shared that back in February and March the district presented the hope to handle some of the deficit with retirement attrition which is not always the best case, but better than the alternative.   The district proposal included a reduction of about 20 secondary teachers, closer to 30 overall. However, the district had 39 to 40 teachers either resigning due to retirement or expiring one-year only contracts allowing to revisit the classroom teacher allocations and para educator allocations.  He provided the Board different scenarios for classroom size changes and ratios. Assistant Superintendent Priddy shared that the district is currently working on the analysis. Because of the complexity to identify where to replace the individuals who have retired or resigned, principals have been working with Human Resources to ensure that the certification of staff aligned with the master schedule expectation of either the middle school or the high school. Using construction funds, the district has been paying for an extra administrator for two (2) schools. As the district move to the final phase of construction at the beginning of the 2021 school year, instead of deploying an extra principal, it will deploy the typical number of principals, but cover some of the costs of one of the principals at each school.  These principals will invest a fair amount in capital programs releasing basic education resources.  There are no plans to fill the following vacated positions: Elementary Assistant Principal, Social Worker, and School Nurse.

  3.4 School Start Time Proposal
Superintendent Murphy provided a general overview of the school start time proposal, similar to the April 20 Board Meeting presentation and the recommendation of Transfinder,  a corporation that develops routing, scheduling and fleet maintenance solutions for optimal transportation logistics. Transfinder looked at the efficiencies of the District’s current system and provided their findings and recommendations on moving the schools start time close to what the Citizen Advisory Committee is recommended.
  As mentioned during the April 20 presentation, Bellingham School District recently changed its school start time.  They reported that the change was fairly seamless planned in a very thoughtful way including community feedback and constant communication throughout the process. Similar to OSD, Bellingham was driven by the adolescent sleep time research that focuses on the importance of student mental health and physical health. They provided information on added cost to their District, impact on school staffing, and mentioned their transportation levy for additional buses and routes. Based on Transfinder’s study and Bellingham’s report, the OSD school day could look different next year.  .
  Superintendent Murphy shared that he has heard and recognize that there are many things that need to be resolved before a significant change to school start times can be made. Postponing the change to a later date, possibly fall 2021 would be ideal. He addressed a question about why Bellingham School District took three (3) years to implement the program, similar to Olympia.  He shared that Bellingham is a bit larger than Olympia and the delay was due to the ground Bellingham had to cover for their bus routes.  Bellingham has ~12,000 students and while making the significant changes in later start times for its high schools, the district simultaneously changed the high school schedule shifting from a 6-period day to eight-period schedule with four periods each day. Olympia is not looking at alternative schedules rather looking exclusively at changing the time and using the same allotted amount of time during the day to run school, whatever that schedule would be.
  Director Huff moved to accept the administration School Start Time Proposal as presented with the implementation to begin fall 2021 and implementing a status report in the winter. Vice-President Clifthorne seconded the motion. Discussion followed.  
  Director Huff led a brief discussion in regard to start time for schools clarifying that no school will start before 8 a.m.  The Board discussed the impact on the bus driver schedule, the shortage of drivers, and moving forward in the process strongly encouraging district administrators to offer and maintain similar work hours for drivers.  When in effect fall 2021, the new start time plan will be reviewed in the winter to determine if revisions need to be made.
  Being no further discussion, the Board unanimously approved the OSD Administration School Start Time Proposal as presented with the implementation to begin fall 2021 and a status report in the winter 2021. No opposition.  Motion passed.
