Reflection on 2023-24 School Improvement Plan Goal (linked here)

Where were we going?

Our School Improvement Plan goal for 2023-24 was to improve the educational outcomes of
Hispanic/Latino, Black/African American students, as well as students receiving IEPs & MLLs and
504 accommodations at MIHS by June 2024. Our goal was to eliminate barriers for these groups of
students by building authentic relationships.

We began focusing on improving student belonging during the 2022-23 school year, following a
survey completed by students in October 2022. Throughout the year, we implemented various
initiatives to help students feel more connected to their teachers, administration, school, and peers.
We promoted inclusion and equity, enhanced flexibility and support, and ensured student voices were
integrated into every decision-making process. Inclusive assemblies were held, and
relationship-building activities such as personal stories, icebreakers, and group work were
incorporated into classrooms. Additionally, we provided professional development on
microaggressions, implicit bias, and fostering courageous conversations. We also increased student
access to administrators and school board members through advisory roles, surveys, and
participation in groups like the Superintendent Student Advisory Committee.

How are we doing? (Quantitative Reflection _2023-24SY)

Our efforts continued last year, resulting in improvements across several key metrics.

According to our 2022-23 EES data, 13% of Black students and 15% of Hispanic students reported
not having an adult they could turn to if they had a problem. By 2023-24, these numbers dropped to
0% for Black students and 5% for Hispanic students. (Celebration)

We observed similar positive trends in other demographics, including ELL, special education, and 504
students. In 2022-23, 8% of English language learners, 6% of special education students, and 12% of
students with 504 accommodations reported not having an adult to talk to. By 2023-24, these
numbers dropped to 4% for special education students and 9% for students with 504
accommodations. (Celebration) However, the number of ELL students who felt they lacked an adult
to confide in increased by 3 points. (Growth area)

The 2022-23 EES data indicated that none of the Black students (0%) reported feeling positive about
coming to school most of the time. However, this increased to 38% in 2023-24! (Celebration) There
was also an improvement, though less significant, in the number of Black students who generally
didn’t feel positive about attending MIHS. In 2022-23, 31% of students reported this feeling, which
decreased to 26% in 2023-24.

Sadly, we did not see the same trend among Hispanic students. Among this student group, 26% of
students generally feel unsatisfied with MIHS in 2023-24, up from 12% in 2022-23. In light of this


data, we will focus a portion of our 2024-25 SIP efforts on identifying what factors are contributing to
the 14% increase in Hispanic students reporting they seldom enjoy MIHS. (Growth area)

Students receiving services showed the most variability in their “I enjoy coming to school” EES data.
In 2022-23, 100% of English language learners reported that they enjoyed coming to school at least
occasionally, but this number dropped to 92% in 2023-24. Students receiving 504 accommodations
remained consistent, with 71% reporting enjoyment across both years. Meanwhile, students receiving
special education services saw a significant increase, with the percentage rising from 81% to 93%
reporting that they enjoy coming to school at least occasionally.

A student's sense of belonging, feeling they have a trusted adult at MIHS, and enjoying coming to
school are closely interconnected, and experts point out that these factors affect their academic
performance. In the table below, we'll take a closer look at our efforts to see if they had an impact on
these students' academic success.

Absenteeism is higher among students with 504 plans, IEP services, as well as Black and Hispanic
students. As a result, students in these subgroups may face additional barriers to attending school
regularly, potentially affecting their academic performance. In 2024-25, we will examine the underlying
causes of absenteeism for these specific groups of students to target interventions that improve
attendance and educational outcomes. (Growth area)

The Trend Data over Time table below shows the percentage of F’s and NC’s earned by each
marginalized group compared to students not in these groups. Over the past four grading periods, as
we focused on increasing the sense of belonging for these, and all, students at MIHS, the data
reveals that multi-language learners made the most significant improvements in their academic
success rate while Black/African American and Hispanic students made modest improvements.
Students receiving services experienced slightly poorer outcomes. (Growth area)

Where to next? How the work of 2023-24 shapes the 2024-25 school year.

With our intentional efforts last year, we made positive impacts on all marginalized groups except
those who receive IEP services. That said, we still have a long way to go. What are our next steps?
