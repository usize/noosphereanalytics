  6. Action
A. Operations - School Capacity and Utilization Task Force Recommendations; Dr. Vincent Pecchia, Assistant Superintendent of Operations and School Support; Brian Devereux, Director of Facilities Planning; Brady Martin, Director of Capital Projects (01:15:18)
Dr. Pecchia explained this is a follow-up report from the last school board meeting on June 3, 2024, and is an action item that reviews identified recommendations to address challenges related to student housing and school capacity utilization within our district. He introduced Brian Devereux, Director of Facilities Planning, to review the SCUT recommendations to the board. Brady Martin, Director of Capital Projects, was also available to address questions from the board if needed.

Director Devereux reminded the board that the Diamond Schools: Dessie Evans, Edgerton, Fruitland, Meeker, Pope, and Puyallup High School are schools projected to be at capacity, or slightly over capacity, within the next four years. Emerald Ridge, Glacier View, Northwood, and Rogers High School are all projected to have enrollment exceeding building capacity within the next four years. While waiver management is recommended for each of these schools, additional mitigation measures were recommended by the task force as listed below.

Ferrucci Junior High and Mt. View Elementary each have sufficient building capacity to serve its projected enrollment. However, these schools are included as part of the recommendations, and thus, are an impacted school.

Northwood Elementary Recommendations
To relieve a projected elementary school capacity deficit on North Hill, the Northwood recommendation includes:
Waiver management at Northwood Elementary and Mt. View Elementary beginning in the 2024-25 school year, AND
Relocate the QUEST program from Northwood to Mt.View in 2025-26, with an option to relocate Kindergarten Academy from Mt. View to Northwood simultaneous with the Quest relocation.

Glacier View Junior High Recommendations
To manage enrollment increases projected within the Glacier View attendance area, the task force recommends:
Waiver management at Glacier View beginning as early as 2024-25, AND
Commission a Boundary Review Committee in 2025-26 to recommend a boundary change between Glacier View and Ferrucci for implementation in the 2026-27 school year.

Emerald Ridge High School Recommendations
At Emerald Ridge, the task force recommends:
Waiver Management at ERHS beginning as early as 2024-25, AND
Conversion of the classroom wing Project Areas to classroom space as early as 2026-27.

Rogers High School Recommendations
At Rogers, the task force recommends:
Waiver Management at RHS beginning as early as 2024-25, AND
The addition of up to 3 portable classrooms for the 2025-26 school year.

The final two recommendations are not site-specific, but rather general in nature. The first general recommendation is to continue the School Capacity and Utilization Task Force as a standing committee and the second is to establish a Bond Advisory Committee. They are asking the board to consider acting on approving the School Capacity Utilization Taskforce recommendation package that includes school-specific mitigation measures, the continuation of SCUT and the establishment of a Bond Advisory Committee.

When asked by the board if the continuation of the committee would include the same members or a new group of individuals, Director Devereux replied they believe they have enough existing members in good standing to fill the spots on the next committee. It would be good to keep that knowledge from the past 14 meetings. We have enough participation indicated in the survey that would be able to fill the spots. There were three high school students included and two are graduating, and one who was a sophomore will be invited back. Director Devereux answered additional questions from the board.
  A motion was made to approve the School Capacity Utilization Taskforce recommendation package that includes school-specific mitigation measures, the continuation of SCUT and the establishment of a Bond Advisory Committee, as presented.
  Motion by Joseph Romero, second NONE.
Final Resolution: Motion Carries
Yea: Joseph Romero, David B Berg, Gretchen S Miles, Shannon C Burch

B. Operations - Water Quality and Conservation Plan; Dr. Vincent Pecchia, Assistant Superintendent of Operations and School Support; Phillip Anderson, Director of Maintenance (01:28:19)
Dr. Vincent Pecchia explained this report will review the district’s partnership with the Washington State Department of Health and action plan that complies with Engrossed Second Substitute House Bill 1129. As a requirement of the bill, the Board of Directors will be asked to consider taking action to approve the district's action plan for testing and remediating lead in the drinking water of schools built or fully re-plumbed before 2016. He is co-presenting this evening with Phillip Anderson, Director of Maintenance.

Engrossed Second Substitute House Bill 1139 mandates the Washington State Department of Health to test for lead in drinking water in public schools constructed before or fully re-plumbed before 2016. The testing must be completed by June 30, 2026, and repeated every five years. The accepted lead level is set at five parts per billion. Director Anderson reviewed the scope of testing for lead in drinking water and the district’s action plan for addressing sinks and faucets that test over five parts per billion.

Director Anderson said the house bill covers sinks and faucets where it is reasonably foreseeable that the water may be used for drinking, cooking, or food preparation. It excludes sinks in restrooms, kitchen sinks used for dishwashing, hand wash sinks, and art and science room sinks. The district is working closely with the Washington State Department of Health to test sinks and faucets for lead in drinking water. Currently, tests are conducted at no cost to the district. These tests are performed by Washington State Department of Health staff before school hours to ensure accuracy before water starts flowing through the pipes. Results are typically returned to the district within two to three weeks. By the end of this month, five of our schools—Carson, Edgerton, Aylen, Kalles, and Glacier View—will have been tested. Additional schools will be tested throughout the 2024-2025 school year.

He described the details of the action plan and said the Maintenance Department is working closely with the Communications Department to effectively convey the district's approach to water quality and conservation, including lead testing and results. This collaboration ensures annual communication with students, families, and staff, posting test results, and detailing ongoing remediation efforts by schools. They offered to answer any questions from the board.

President Romero made several statements about unfunded mandates and said we need to continue to ask for more money from the state, and he wants to make sure the public is aware that we have to take community levy dollars to cover unfunded mandates. Dr. Polm added it is important to mention that we continue to partner with other entities because while we can control the pipes and faucets within our school buildings we cannot control some of the other pipes and equipment that might be very old. Director Anderson agreed and said this is the first leg of the five-year testing so in its infancy stage. They are doing a good job of mapping out the fixtures that need to be tested. We will see how those tests come back and will learn a lot in the next couple years.

Director Berg agreed it is important to remind our legislators of their paramount duty to fully fund education and not use our levy dollars. The Board supports the work they are doing but wants to hold the state accountable.
  A motion was made to approve the district's water quality and conservation action plan, as presented.
  Motion by Gretchen S Miles, second NONE.

  C. Equity and Instructional Leadership - Second Reading and Action of 2024-25 Student Rights and Responsibilities Handbook; Dr. Gordon Brobbey, Director of Cultural Competency, Equity, Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (CCDEI) (01:39:22)
Dr. Brobbey reminded the board that he and his colleague Dr. Jamie Lee, Executive Director of Categorical Programs and School Support led the work of revising and updating the Student Rights and Responsibilities Handbook for the upcoming 2024-2025 school year. Tonight they shared information regarding the purpose, process, and some highlights of their work. The work of revising the Rights and Responsibilities Handbook aligns with the first three of the five Priority Areas identified in the District's Strategic Plan, i.e., (1) Academic Achievement, (2) Family and Community Engagement, and (3) Social-Emotional Supports.

The review of the handbook served three critical purposes:
To ensure it is reflective of recent changes in state law and policies, examples of which will be shown shortly.
To include issued recommendations from the Office of Superintendent of Public Instruction (OSPI), and
To ensure that the handbook language is consistent with best practices in school discipline.

To ensure that the work reflects the voices of buildings and programs, administrators from the district office, and all three school levels were invited to join a committee that was constituted. Dr. Alicia Nosworthy and Gus Wessel served as district representatives. Elementary buildings were represented by Waller Road Elementary Assistant Principal Michael Braun, Edgerton Elementary Assistant Principal Dina Slater, and Hunt Elementary Principal, Kris Diamond. Suzanne Cella, Assistant Principal of Ballou JH, Jaison Naiker, Assistant Principal at Ferrucci JH, Stephanie Clarno, Principal at Ferrucci JH, and Chris Hunke, Dean of Students at Ballou JH represented the JH level, while Dr. Angelina Quiles, Assistant Principal at ERHS, and Mark Barnes, PHS Assistant Principal joined for the high schools. Kim Ledger, Director of Special Education provided vital input from the special education lens.

The Committee met three times in the month of May. He shared highlights of revisions that have been made. Throughout the document, minor revisions as editing and correcting of spelling errors were made. In other instances, however, they clarified or updated expectations and language. On pages 5 & 6, for example, we clarified expectations around Activity Cards and homework.

As referenced on previous slides, OSPI recently issued a model handbook language around HIB, discrimination, sexual harassment, and gender-inclusive schools. It was informed by House Bill 1207, now codified into law as RCW 28A:300.286, requiring all school districts to include the model language in student handbook for the upcoming school year. The model language also includes information on the overlap between nondiscrimination and HIB as well as information on complaint procedures and district contacts for the benefit of students and their families. The exact language, along with information on applicable district policies and coordinators’ contact information has been added to the handbook and can be found on pages 9-12.

In Section II, we updated terminology around Student Discipline Terms to reflect recent change in language. They deleted specific expectations that were built around COVID-19. Additionally, updated timelines related to planned absences.

Upon board approval, a PDF version of the Rights and Responsibilities for the next school year will immediately be posted to our website. However, new this year, the Communications Department will be digitalizing all the content and creating a webpage that is fully accessible and translatable. That work is expected to be completed this summer.

It is our plan to continue to engage the committee in ongoing discussions with the purpose of demonstrating proactivity in response to updates in student support policies and practices. We also plan to engage in ongoing discussions around trends in discipline, including how best to support schools. Finally, our plan for next year will include potential workshop series on parental support.

He asked if the board had any questions. Director Berg said he appreciates the work and mentioned our student reps past comments about students not always realizing the protections that are available to them so he appreciates the work by the Communications Department to make this more accessible to students. President Romero agreed and said he also appreciates the efforts to make it more accessible.
  A motion was made to approve the 2024-25 Student Rights and Responsibilities Handbook, as presented.
