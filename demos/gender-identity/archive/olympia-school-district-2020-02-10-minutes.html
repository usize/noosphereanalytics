  3.3 Regional Transportation Plan – Thurston Regional Planning Council (TRPC)
Marc Daily, Executive Director and Katrina Van Every, Senior Planner provided an overview of TRPC’s “What Moves You” Regional Transportation Plan.  The plan is a roadmap that provides an overall analysis of how transportation will work in our region over a 20-25-year timeframe and supports coordination among jurisdictions. The plan also acts as an important tool in meeting state and federal transportation requirements, ensuring continued funding from these sources.  The plan identifies those projects and issues that change the way traffic flows throughout the region, complementing the local planning that makes the roadway network function within each jurisdiction. The plan addresses the diversity within the region – rural communities, city centers, and outlying suburban neighborhoods. School districts play an important role as well. Each has different issues and priorities as well as common needs and objectives.  Maximizing the benefits of all transportation modes (transit, cycling, walking, personal vehicles) is most efficiently accomplished at a regional scale.  Mr. Daily provided information on upcoming capacity and new connection projects. TRPC’s Community Survey is currently out in the community asking Thurston County residents to provide thoughts on the types of high capacity transit options; transportation related greenhouse gas reduction; the importance of different types of transportation investments; and the impacts and importance of emerging transportation technologies over the next 25 years. The survey is expected to close on the February 28th. Results will be available in April and action will be taken in July.
3.4 Cultural Access Taxes
Patty Belmonte, Director of Hands on Children Museum provided a brief history about the Cultural Access Taxes Bill passed in July 2015. The purpose of the bill was to allow creation of a local program that will have public and educational benefits and provide economic support for cultural organizations throughout the state.  This would also allow to create the opportunity to address communities' diminished access to cultural experiences and/or education programs for K-12 students in public schools. The Washington State legislature granted local governments the authority to create a Cultural Access Program in their County. Revenue raised from a sales tax (or property tax in Counties other than King), is used to strengthen access to cultural, science, and heritage organizations and their activities, as well as enhance citizens' participation in their cultural events and programs. Any County in Washington State may choose to create a Cultural Access Program.  However, should a County decline to create a Cultural Access Program, a city within that County may create such a program implemented within city boundaries. Cultural organizations can be included such as heritage facilities, children’s museums, science centers, fine arts, zoos, aquariums, theaters, art museums, and performing arts.  The public approved funding can be used for reduced or free admission; providing education experiences at an organization’s facilities or in the schools; expanding opportunities for diverse and underserved populations and communities; creating experiences outside an organization’s own facilities; increasing public access through technology; and building capacity of community based organizations including capital needs. 
Ms. Belmonte explained that with hard work, community support, and campaigning, a year and a half later to be able to pass their tax, the City of Tacoma has been the most successful for creating art access for city residents. It was a grassroots effort where the City listened to the community, designed a program to meet the needs, allocated funds, and built capacity.  The City of Spokane, San Juan and King Counties are working to create a Cultural Access Program for their community.  Legislation pending at the state level to modify the bill.  The revision would only affect King County and should not have an impact on what may be happening in Olympia. 
  3.5 McKinney-Vento – Domenico Spatolla-Knoll and Leslie Gowell (OSD)
 Wrap Around Services/City Homeless Response – Keith Stahley (City of Olympia)
Principal of Madison Elementary, Domenico Spatolla-Knoll and Leslie Gowell, Homeless Program K-12, provided a brief overview about the McKinney Vento Act that was signed into legislation in 1987 to address homelessness in the United States and information about student homeless population at the Olympia School District. The primary goal of McKinney Vento assistance is to provide students access to public education and school stability so they have the opportunity to experience academic success and feel belonging within the school community.
The OSD McKinney-Vento K-12 Program priority is to provide student support and get them connected with district resources such as transportation, child nutrition, special education, preschool, tutoring, after school and athletic programs so they can regularly and fully participate in school. For many of these students by virtue of their temporary housing or lack of housing, regular school attendance can be difficult. At the same time however, a school can serve as the place of greatest stability in a student’s life. The District’s strives to help prevent students from needing to change schools over the course of a school year. Research shows that changing schools during the school year can result in four to six months of lost academic progress which impacts on-time grade level progression.
Student families are often forced to double up due to loss of rental property, eviction, workplace injuries, job loss, reduction in income, unexpected medical bills, divorce or domestic violence. Within the District boundaries there are 36 shelter beds for family members and 20 domestic violence shelter beds for victims and their student children. Unaccompanied Youth is another subcategory who are eligible and defined as students who are not in the physical custody of a parent or legal guardian due to leaving home to due extreme conflict or were put out by their parents. Unaccompanied youth can also be students who were left in the care of a friend or relative due their parents being unable provide for their basic needs.
  Out of the 10,000 students enrolled in the Olympia School District, 272 students or 2.5% of the population were identified as McKinney Vento eligible. The overall population was split in half with 50% being elementary age students and the other half being 6th-12th graders. The preschool population is low. Preschoolers are typically identified when there are school age siblings who are enrolling or enrolled and also tend to be the children of highly mobile families. In the 2019-2020 school year, 155 identified McKinney Vento students enrolled in the district.
  McKinney Vento students often face common barriers that prevent them to be able to access public education and participate in schools.  Fortunately, Olympia School District offers resources to provide students with options to lessen these barriers.  The District provides school supplies & limited assistance with emergency school clothing. Students are automatically eligible for free lunch and breakfast. The District partners with the Thurston County Food Bank and the Homeless backpacks organization for weekend food bags.  School buildings assist McKinney-Vento students with washing their clothes at school and some buildings have the capacity to allow them to shower at school. The District also have a laundry voucher program with the Westside laundry.  Also, a Tech Levy passed in 2018 provides students the opportunity to have access to a Chromebook and hotspot for being able to do homework after school. The Boys and Girls Club of Olympia located at Jefferson Middle School offers students such a place to socialize, play and do homework.  The Olympia School District is fortunate to have a staff of dedicated professionals who work hard to support and help McKinney Vento students overcome barriers so that they can fully participate in school.  Principal Spatola-Knoll shared a real life success story about a McKinney-Vento Olympia School District student, Angel.   
  Keith Stahley, Interim City Manager announced that city’s Community Work Group will be handing off the group’s work and process to the Olympia City Council during their meeting, tomorrow, February 11th at 5:30 p.m. in the Council Chambers.  The workgroup will take the opportunity to share their experience and what they have learned over the years about the process and implementing a community wide response to homelessness. The group discovered that there are multiple issues and the city needs to take the necessary risks in the process in order to provide the results hoped for.  A regional response is needed and should include an organized coordinated entry system; affordable housing; homeless prevention; and the increase of public health and safety. Many staff and community partners were engaged in different focus groups helping to streamline a response and wrap around services. The city is in the process of establishing a second mitigation site, but currently do not have the social services support at this time.

Frank Wilson, Director of Operations reported that the district’s new construction projects are equipped with materials to help reduce carbon footprint.  He shared the following on new construction:
The use of solar power and installation of naturally dimming lights;
Installation and use of LED products;
Installation of a rainwater harvesting system for toilet flushing;
Installation of low flow fixtures;
Installation of variable refrigerant flow (various schools/OHS);
Installation of ductless heating system to prevent heat loss.

    3.7 Impact Fees
Assistant Superintendent, Jennifer Priddy reported on the impact fees formula basics noting that the formula is governed by the Growth Management Act in an attempt to capture the cost of growth or the cost of housing more students.  The attempt also excludes the cost to upgrade facilities with no anticipated increase in students housed.  Also, to give property owners credit for state grants that the district will receive and give owners credit for the taxes they are going to pay over the life of upcoming bond payments.  In the formula, it is the Districts authority for flexibility lies in the discount rate.  The District discount rate is 15%.
  The District’s Capital Facilities Plan (CFP) highlight in detail school building projects included in the calculation of the impact fee.  Major projects driving the plan are construction at
Capital High School, Avanti High School, and Olympia High School, as well as the continued remodel of the two (2) elementary schools (which include the mini-buildings), and the
future potential of a 6th mini-building.
  Actual Calculation is more complex.  The formula is actually six (6) calculations of simplified example: a) by grade (elementary, middle and high school); b) by type of new housing (single family and multifamily). There is a separate calculation that adds to the impact fee if the district will spend money on portables. There is also a separate calculation that adds to the impact fee if the district must acquire land in order to build a facility.
  Both City and District staff agree that finding time to have a conversation to discuss the impact fee and simplifying the process is necessary.
