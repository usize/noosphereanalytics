Executive Summary:

Achievement in Results-2 continues to be overall high and there are some bright spots with some student
subgroups showing positive achievement and trends.

R2.1 — Literacy and Language: The 2023-24 literacy data for grades 3, 8, and 10 highlights areas of strength
and opportunities for growth. Grade 3 students demonstrated consistent proficiency across multiple
indicators, though disparities persist for students receiving Special Education services, multilingual learners,
and students from low-income households. While literacy performance at grades 8 and 10 remains above
state averages, there has been a decline in state assessment results since 2019, reflecting broader post-
pandemic trends. To address these challenges, we are implementing a multi-faceted literacy strategy. This
includes the UFLI Foundations pilot in K-2 classrooms, structured literacy professional learning for educators,
and data-driven small group instruction tailored to student needs. Multilingual and Special Education
teachers are receiving targeted support and resources to address specific literacy development needs.
Secondary efforts focus on aligning ELA curriculum implementation and increasing collaboration among
grade-level teams to ensure consistent, research-based instruction. These initiatives aim to close
achievement gaps and ensure that all students achieve literacy success

R2.2 — Math: The 2023-24 mathematics data reveals notable strengths and persistent challenges across
grades 5, 8, and 10. Performance on state assessments, universal screeners, and classroom grades
underscores overall proficiency, but significant gaps remain for students receiving Special Education
services, multilingual learners, students from low-income households, and some racial and ethnic groups.
While post-pandemic declines in math performance are evident, these declines are less severe in the district
compared to state trends. Cohort data further highlights areas where certain student groups have struggled
to sustain growth, particularly in middle school and among multilingual learners.

To address these challenges, the district has adopted a multi-faceted approach to improve math instruction.
Building Thinking Classrooms strategies are being integrated to promote active engagement and critical
thinking, while instructional resources like Illustrative Mathematics are aligned to foster inquiry-based
learning. Professional learning emphasizes small-group instruction tailored to individual student needs,
informed by data from tools like Dreambox and FastBridge. Special Education and multilingual learners
benefit from targeted scaffolding, including pre-teaching vocabulary and fostering mathematical discourse.
At the secondary level, middle school math pathways have been restructured to ensure equitable access to
rigorous coursework, supported by ongoing professional development and collaboration for teachers. These
efforts are designed to close gaps, elevate achievement, and ensure all students are equipped to succeed in
mathematics

R2.3 —- Science: The 2023-24 science data highlights a mix of strengths and areas for growth across grades 5,
8, and 10-11. Grade 5 proficiency levels are consistent across state assessments and report card grades,
though gaps remain for multilingual learners, students from low-income households, and students receiving
Special Education services. Grade 8 results show lower proficiency on state assessments compared to
classroom grades, with declines since 2019, though the district outperforms state averages. At the high
school level, most students are earning passing grades in science courses and meeting credit requirements,
but disparities persist among demographic groups.

To address these challenges, the district has implemented targeted strategies to strengthen science
instruction. Elementary teachers continue to use Amplify Science, integrating science with literacy and social
studies to build coherence and background knowledge. At the secondary level, the district has adopted
OpenSciEd as the core curriculum for middle school science, emphasizing hands-on, standards-based
learning that is particularly effective for underserved student groups. Professional learning and collaboration
for teachers focus on aligning instruction with best practices, including scaffolds for multilingual learners
such as pre-teaching vocabulary and using visual supports. These efforts aim to deepen student engagement
in science and ensure equitable access to high-quality learning opportunities.

Superintendent Interpretation:

Students will have the skills and knowledge to perform at or above grade level proficiency in Reading, Writing,
Listening, Speaking and Language Skills and apply those skills to develop competencies that lead to academic,
professional, and personal success.

Learning to read is the “epicenter of equity” (Zaretta Hammond). Based on the NAEP Reading assessment (2022),
66% of 4th graders are reading below a proficient level, which puts them at a higher risk of high school dropout and
ending up on welfare or in prison. As directed by RCW 28A.320.203, Washington’s schools and districts are required
to identify students’ reading levels according to evidence-based state and district selected assessments in grades K-
4. If students are identified as reading “below grade level standard”, the RCW states that districts must provide
reading interventions to improve the reading skills of these students. By 3rd grade, students should have mastered
the necessary foundational skills to transfer from “learning to read” to “reading to learn”. Focusing on 3rd grade
indicators and targets provides a picture of Lake Washington School District’s progress towards meeting the outlined
competencies.

8th grade ELA student data is essential to understanding the effectiveness of instructional practices in the middle
grades because these students have been fully immersed in their learning experiences at this level for nearly three
full years. Measuring indicators at the 8th grade level allows instructional leaders to examine the data that provides
us with essential longitudinal competency data for our students for each of those grade levels before they head to
the high school level.

In 10th grade, our students take their final SBA or WA-AIM state assessment. This is an important data point because
it is an indicator for graduation and post-secondary success in ELA and success in other content areas. Analyzing data
at this grade level helps to identify students who may need additional support to reach these essential goals. In
addition to the option that 11th and 12th graders have to take the SBA in order to meet graduation pathway
requirements, it is essential that this grade level data is included as part of our high school ELA indicators to
understand the strengths and needs of our program as it prepares students for post-secondary success.

e Demonstrating independence: Students can, without significant scaffolding, comprehend and evaluate
complex texts across a range of types and disciplines, and they can construct effective arguments and
convey intricate or multifaceted information.

e Building strong content knowledge: Students establish a base of knowledge across a wide range of subject
matter by engaging with works of quality and substance. Students become proficient in new areas through
research and study. Students read purposefully and listen attentively to gain both general knowledge and
discipline-specific expertise. Students refine and share their knowledge through writing and speaking.

e Responding to the varying demands of audience, task, purpose, and discipline: Students adapt their
communication in relation to audience, task, purpose, and discipline. Students set and adjust purpose for
reading, writing, speaking, listening, and language use as warranted by the task.

e Comprehending as well as critiquing: Students are engaged and open-minded—but discerning— readers
and listeners. Students work diligently to understand precisely what an author or speaker is saying, but they
also question an author’s or speaker’s assumptions and premises and assess the veracity of claims and the
soundness of reasoning.

e Valuing evidence: Students cite specific evidence when offering an oral or written interpretation of a text.
Students use relevant evidence when supporting their own points in writing and speaking, making their
reasoning clear to the reader or listener, and they constructively evaluate others’ use of evidence.

How We Are Responding in 2024-25: Systemic Work

We continue our work at all levels of our system in alignment with our focus on Inclusion, Multi-tiered Systems of
Support (MTSS), and Equity. All schools are required to set academic goals as part of the school improvement
planning process and to implement strategies for groups of students in the school who are not performing at grade
level. Building leaders use the Fidelity Integrity Assessment tool (FIA) to identify areas of strengths and needs in
multiple areas of MTSS, including inclusive, high-quality, and impactful academic instruction.

Across all levels, the district has continued a deep focus improving student outcomes by strengthening and
diversifying Tier 1 instruction in classrooms. In 2024-25, the district infused learning about and usage of LWSD’s
Instructional Playbook in a variety of new ways to support educators in effectively implementing these critical
instructional practices that accelerate student learning. The Professional Learning department incorporated short
videos of LWSD classrooms into the online Playbook resources to help educators see these strategies in action
locally; principals were provided with microlearning resources to help them model Tier 1 strategies consistently in
their buildings; and the team partnered with New Teacher Support Program and MTSS Coaches to frame coaching
cycles around these strategies.

Lake Washington is also leveraging Washington State’s new Student Growth Goal (SGG) process to strengthen
instructional practices and specifically address disproportionality in student outcomes. In 2024-25, LWSD educators
received 6 hours of professional learning about the new SGG process, focused on core tenets such as the importance
of fostering students’ cognitive and emotional engagement. To integrate and continue prior learning, this
professional development highlighted specific Tier 1 strategies from the Instructional Playbook that address the

Full time MTSS Instructional coaches are in each elementary school to partner directly with the building principal in
support of building goals and to provide professional learning to educators using a Transformational coaching
model. We have assigned a Principal on Special Assignment to develop the skill sets of the coaches and support
principals to utilize them effectively, with a focus on coaching centered on transformation, culturally responsive
instruction, and tiered student supports. Elementary building administrators meet regularly with the POSA in
collaboration and consultation to facilitate a culture for coaching in their building.

support. Coaches are a flexible resource in the buildings and duties can include meeting with professional learning
communities (PLC), co-facilitate MTSS-I pre-referral team at their buildings by implementing an effective process,
modeling and guiding strengths-based conversations, supporting data analysis and identification and
implementation of tiered supports.

To best meet the diverse and changing needs of the district, coaches develop expertise in specific content and
develop liaisons with professional learning specialists to fully understand and support the effective implementation
of our district adopted curricular tools, and the pedagogy necessary to plan and implement instruction for all
students.

At the secondary level, district Specialists facilitate meetings with department chairs to focus on leadership
development, system alignment (including common course descriptions), collective problem solving, leadership
skills, coherence in practice and policy, and improving capacity to promote high quality instruction in their school
teams. Teachers are provided opportunities for learning and collaboration for high-quality instruction during
Teacher Professional Work (TPW) time. This has included teams working on evaluating and adjusting the scope and
sequence of the middle school ELA curriculum, as well as multiple sessions on diverse topics. In addition to the
specific topics of learning sessions, essential goals of professional learning include offering a variety of research-
based instructional strategies that support deep understanding, decrease teacher isolation, and increase
effectiveness of Tier 1 teaching strategies for the benefit of all students.

How We Are Responding in 2024-25: Literacy Focus

We continue a multi-pronged approach to support students reading at grade level by the end of third grade that
includes professional learning, data informed instruction and use of evidence-based resources and strategies with
students who are at risk for reading difficulty including dyslexia. Multiple departments/divisions are focused on
aligning this work to ensure strongest impact on student learning, specific to literacy. We continue to refine the
skills and resources teachers need to teach to provide each student with direct explicit instruction in phonics and
phonemic awareness. Teachers have indicated a need for additional resources and professional learning to support
small group instruction during the daily reading blocks. One element of our multipronged response to this need

includes implementing the University of Florida Literacy Institute (UFLI) Foundations resources in kindergarten

The primary purpose of UFLI is to support educators with understanding literacy research and moving from theory
into classroom practice. K5 Teaching and Learning continues to deliver UFLI Foundations professional learning to
buildings as part of a comprehensive pilot, in collaboration with Multilingual and Safety Net Programs. The
professional learning shares background knowledge about UFLI Foundations, how to implement the resource, and
its importance in supporting small group instruction in the classroom setting. It also connects the principles and
components of UFLI Foundations to classroom application. Participants use data to group students according to
where reading acceleration is needed. Each lesson step is modeled, and teachers have time to collaborate, explore
resources, request additional support, consider how to begin implementation, and practice routines and skills to
effectively implement UFLI Foundations. The goal of this is that all students’ proficiency in the foundational skill of
phonics will increase, which will increase the number of students reading at grade level by the end of third grade.
The results from this year’s pilot will inform next steps in implementation.

Another element of our multipronged approach is the continuation of Structured Literacy professional learning
focused on the teaching of phonics, phonemic awareness, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension to support
student learning. The focus has been on cohorts of teachers new to Lake Washington School District including Safety
Net, Multilingual, Special Education, and K-2 classroom teachers as well as some third-grade teachers who have
students who need continued direct explicit instruction in these skills.

All K5 classrooms are expected to teach reading in a block that includes whole group and small group instruction.
The focus of support and professional learning for teachers has been how to determine what small groups of
students need in terms direct and explicit foundational skills instruction. This is accomplished using student data and
curriculum resources aligned to those identified student needs.

Individual student FastBridge reading data is reviewed by grade level teachers in elementary and middle schools
through a data process facilitated by the Safety Net teachers at each school at the end of each benchmark period.
Students who demonstrate risk for reading difficulties, including dyslexia, participate in additional assessments to
determine the discrete reading skill to focus on in small group instruction with evidence-based curriculum resources
and instructional routines.

Special Education and Multilingual (ML) support is provided in multiple ways. ML and Special Education teachers are
participating in UFLI training with their buildings, with continued structured literacy training provided for new ML
teachers. Additionally, ML and Special Education teachers engage in targeted, on-going professional learning. For
ML teachers, this has included engaging in the WIDA Writing for Multilingual Learners workshop and a self-paced
training for all teachers, concentrating on the first five components of SIOP (Lesson Preparation, Building
Background, Comprehensible Input, Strategies, and Interaction), along with co-teaching training, to foster teacher
collaboration.

ML teachers are expected to provide literacy support for students, with several teachers implementing this support
through co-teaching across various grade levels. ML teachers continuously review student data, including WIDA,
Fastbridge, SBA, and classroom data, to better determine appropriate levels of support. The program is prioritizing
pull-out instruction for entering and emerging students to provide targeted support. At the secondary level, entering
and emerging students are served in stand-alone classes, while all other ML students receive support in their
mainstream content classes through teacher collaboration, push-in support, and co-teaching. To further enhance
their instructional skills, ML teachers will attend the Washington Association for Bilingual Education (WABE)
conference.

2.2 Mathematical Reasoning and Practices

2.2.1 Students will become increasingly proficient in mathematics, in alignment with the Common Core State
Standards. This includes:
Learning math concepts.
Developing math reasoning.
Developing problem solving and skills fluency.
Understanding and responding to mathematical information and challenges that are part of daily life,
society, and the workplace.

Superintendent Interpretation:

Students must have the skills and knowledge to perform at or above grade level proficiency in Mathematics on state
assessments and nationally normed assessments.

5th grade is a key point in a student’s mathematical learning as it is where mathematical understanding shifts from
arithmetic focus to include concepts of algebra. Using 5th grade data gives us an understanding of the effectiveness
of the primary program through the state testing that occurs at regular intervals until 10th grade. Analyzing this data
at 5th grade us the ability to evaluate the impact of instruction in math and modify as needed at a key grade level in
order to support high levels of achievement in middle school and high school. “Mathematically powerful
spaces...engage each and every student in meaningful mathematical work, cultivate positive mathematical identities,
and develop children as doers and sense-makers of mathematics.” (Catalyzing Change in Elementary School Math,
NCTM, 2020, p. 11)

8th grade math student data is essential to understanding the effectiveness of instructional practices in the middle
grades because it gives a picture of the full middle school experience. Measuring indicators at the 8th grade level
allows instructional leaders to understand the impact of program decisions and structures across the whole middle
school. Looking at 8th grade data provides us with essential longitudinal competency data and allows us to track
which students are accessing Algebra 1 by the end of middle school. Algebra 1 is a foundational course for success in
high school math and science. Students taking Algebra 1 in middle school open the door for taking high level math
courses such as AP Calculus and AP Statistics by their senior year and expands access to AP science and higher level
CTE classes as well. While colleges are broadening their entrance requirements, students taking high level math
courses in high school may be more prepared for college than those who do not. In the unique environment of
middle school, “Each and every student should develop deep mathematical understanding, understand and critique
the world through mathematics, and experience the wonder, joy, and beauty of mathematics, which all contribute
to a positive mathematical identity.” Catalyzing Change in Middle School Math, NCTM, 2020, P. 7.

In 10th grade, our students take the final SBA or WA-AIM state assessment. By 10th grade, many students have
completed the graduation requirements in math and are making more individual choices. 10th grade gives us a final
opportunity to evaluate our overall program with large-scale data. Analyzing results can help identify any persistent
gaps in the middle and high school programs. Examining 10th grade math data gives educators a real opportunity to
respond to the needs of our young adults before they leave our system.

How We Are Responding in 2024-25: Mathematics Focus

To support student growth in mathematics we have focused on providing teachers with access to professional
learning on Building Thinking Classrooms (BTC) by Peter Liljedahl. This approach to learning in mathematics supports
the growth of students as mathematicians by fostering an environment where active engagement and critical
thinking are prioritized. In such classrooms, students are encouraged to explore mathematical concepts through
problem-solving, collaboration, and open-ended questioning. This approach helps them develop a deeper
understanding of the material, rather than just memorizing formulas and procedures. By promoting a culture of
curiosity and resilience, thinking classrooms empower students to take ownership of their learning, build confidence
in their abilities, and cultivate a lifelong love for mathematics. To this end, teachers are able to participate in book
studies, attend conferences both in and outside of our District, and engage in job embedded learning focused on
math with coaching cycles and other learning facilitated by MTSS coaches. To be explicit with the connection
between BTC and Illustrative Mathematics (IM) we are collaborating with a group of elementary grade level teachers
to align mathematics tasks in IM with BTC pedagogy. These resources will be shared with all elementary teachers
through the Curriculum Cloud.

Small group instruction in elementary mathematics has been a focus of professional learning. We are working
directly with teachers to share current research and best practices on how to set up and facilitate small group
learning in mathematics. During this professional learning, we look at student data and use district resources,
including Dreambox and FastBridge, to provide teachers with tools to ensure students receive instruction that will
accelerate their learning in mathematics.

The assumption that “Math is a universal language and therefore accessible regardless of literacy in English...” has
been disproven. Mathematical problem solving and reasoning have a close connection to language (Colorin
Colorado, "Math Instruction for ELLs"). Since the adoption of Illustrative Math for elementary, there has been a
greater focus on inquiry-based practices that require more interaction for students. This interaction asks students to
engage with all of their language domains (speaking, listening, reading, and writing). Today’s math instruction asks
students to collaborate and provide responsive feedback to one another (WIDA Focus Bulletin, 2022). While
mathematical computations can have universal applications, math instruction uses more content and academic
language, which require explicit teaching of academic language in math (Education Week, 2023). There are also
differences in math computations along with background knowledge and context for application. Additionally, the
tools used to measure progress are not solely assessing mathematical computation but also ask students to read,
write, and explain — all things which require English language acquisition. Our data shows that students who were
previously served in ML but have since exited are consistently outperforming their peers who have never been
eligible for ML services.

We continue to support math instruction for students with IEPs and multilingual learners (ML) through multiple
strategies and scaffolding. Special Education and ML teachers are participating in professional development sessions

focused on research-based strategies for math instruction. These strategies include providing access to
mathematical language and vocabulary, building background knowledge, and providing scaffolds for students to
engage in academic discourse with peers. Additionally, the program emphasizes the importance of pre-teaching

math vocabulary, using visuals and hands-on tools, and connecting math problems to real-world experiences

ML teachers continuously review student data, including WIDA, Fastbridge, SBA, and classroom data, to better

encouraging students to explain their thinking, describe their solutions, and present their mathematical ideas.

At the secondary level, district Specialists facilitate meetings with department chairs to focus on leadership

increasing effectiveness of Tier 1 teaching strategies for the benefit of all students. Improving Tier 1 instructional
practices is designed to support students who may not thrive in a traditional math classroom. Adding regular
collaborative discourse strategies to the classroom experience allows students to interact with ideas in multiple
ways- including communicating with multiple different peers about ideas. Additionally, there has been an emphasis
on increasing understanding of the full extent of the Common Core standards and offering a variety of research-

designed third+ year math courses to provide options other than and/or beyond Algebra 2. We continue to provide
support for the expansion of co-teaching in the area of math for Safety Net and Special Education and for summer
programming to support students.

We have implemented the restructuring of our middle school math pathways, which will give all students increased
access to higher levels of rigor. Math teachers are being provided with professional learning, instructional
resources, and collaboration opportunities to support these shifts in implementing effective mathematics teaching
practices centering student voice and agency. District Specialists continue to provide professional learning and
collaboration opportunities in content areas and in instruction to support the effective use of instructional strategies
that are responsive to student need at the building level.

school, and beyond. Participation in quality science instruction from a young age helps develop favorable
attitudes towards science, problem solving skills, and scientific sense-making. Assessing science at the end
of elementary, middle, and high school assists in monitoring the development of students’ scientific
literacy

Superintendent Interpretation:
Students must have the skills and knowledge to perform at or above grade level proficiency in Science on state
assessments and nationally normed assessments.

5th grade is a key point in a student’s scientific learning as it is the first time state testing occurs in science and
gives valuable information about the impact of instruction in science before students transition to middle school.
Analyzing this data at 5th grade supports gives us the ability to evaluate the impact of instruction in science and to
modify as needed at a key grade level in order to support high levels of achievement and engagement in learning
science in middle school.

8th grade science student data is essential to understanding the effectiveness of instructional practices in the
middle grades because it gives a picture of the full middle school experience. Measuring indicators at the 8th
grade level allows instructional leaders to understand the impact of program decisions and structures across the
whole middle school. Looking at 8th grade data provides us with essential longitudinal competency data and
allows us to track which students where students are in their understanding of science standards and their
readiness to enter a more diverse program at high school, including access to AP science and higher level CTE
classes. While colleges are broadening their entrance requirements, students taking high level science courses in
high school may be more prepared for college than those who do not.

Examining 10th and 11th grade data for science achievement provides a more thorough picture of success in
science and students being on-track towards graduation. There are multiple courses that students can take to
fulfill science requirements; looking at grades in any science course taken in 10th grade serves as a proxy for

overall achievement. Tracking credit accumulation in science by the end of 11th grade allows us to identify any
potential issues with students achieving necessary credits for graduation and take steps to support the student/s
and adjust the overall program.

(77.2%) (Tables 21-22). Demographic patterns are relatively consistent across the two grade 5 indicators.

from the target than other comparable groups. There is a positive annual trend for both the state assessment and
report card grades for students receiving Special Education services. O of 14 demographic groups ranked either 1°
or 2" in district benchmark rankings for the state assessment (Table 21).

The Grade 5 Science state assessment performance decreased from pre-pandemic spring 2019 to spring of 2024
by 1.9% (81.7% to 79.8%). There was an increase over that time period for the whole state was by 1.9% (53.0% to
54.9%).

Grade 8 Science Data Summary
The percent proficiency for grade 8 students across the two indicators is varied. Proficiency on the state

assessment for all students is 70.4% (Table 23) while report card grades of C or higher is at 88.1% (Table 24). The
same demographic patterns from grade 5 are also seen in grade 8. 9 of 14 demographic groups ranked either 1°
or 2" in district benchmark rankings for the state assessment (Table 23).

The Grade 8 Science state assessment performance decreased from pre-pandemic spring 2019 to spring of 2024
by 7.2% (77.6% to 70.4%). The decrease over that time period for the whole state was 11.4% (51.4% to 40.0%).

Grade 10-11 Science Data Summary
88.1% of students received a C or higher in their 10" grade science course (Table 25). Demographic patterns are

students eligible for multilingual services, and students from low-income households).

are similar to other indicators.

How We Are Responding in 2024-25: Systemic Work

How We Are Responding in 2024-25: Science Focus

Elementary teachers continue to implement Amplify Science in kindergarten through fifth grade. Building
background knowledge of our students in science is critical in ensuring they have the necessary knowledge and
skills for future success in science as they matriculate through our system. We are working to provide schools with
additional science kits so they can more easily integrate the content of science, social studies and literacy. This
approach to content integration is strongly recommended by OSPI, as it builds coherence in learning for students.

At the elementary level, specific to science, ML teachers are asked to pre-teach vocabulary, provide sentence
stems, and include visuals to support access to content for ML students.

Secondary science teachers had expanded opportunities for collaboration and professional learning that focused
on standards-based learning, using the OSPI-developed open educational resource, OpenSciEd. OpenSciEd
instructional materials are grounded in current research and support high quality science learning for all students
at all levels. The storyline approach and sensemaking embedded into these materials have been shown to be
particularly effective for learners who have been historically underserved. OpenSciEd was moved from being an
