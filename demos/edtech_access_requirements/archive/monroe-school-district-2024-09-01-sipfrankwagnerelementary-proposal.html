Student Populations

1. What key takeaways does your school have about how student groups are performing on state and locally determined, school-level,
indicators of learning and teaching success (e.g., Washington School Improvement Framework)?

Our school has faced ongoing challenges with low performance for an extended period. Notably, our building includes a Spanish Dual
Language program that serves over half of our student population; however, this program has lacked district-wide support until last
school year. While we have seen pockets of growth among students, there has been no systematic approach to build upon these
successes across different grade level teams. Additionally, the high turnover of teaching staff has historically affected the school
culture, resulting in a shortage of trusted adults for some of our most vulnerable students.

2. What are areas of strength your team identified? What data supported them and how can they be leveraged for areas of improvement?
Areas of Strength Identified by Our Team:

1. No Students in the Red Category (iReady): For the first time, we have no students in the red category, as measured by
iReady in both Math and Reading at Kindergarten to 2nd grade at the end of the 23-24 school year. This is a significant
achievement, indicating that all students are within 2 years of their grade level, with none falling behind by more than 2 years.



Section 4: Comprehensive Needs Assessment (Component #1 - Needs Assessment Summary)

2. Improvement in Growth Percentiles: Our growth percentiles have shown marked improvement, with Reading increasing
from 45% to 48% and Math from 38% to 51%, as reported on our OSPI report card.

Data Supporting These Strengths:

e iReady Assessments: The absence of students in the red category is directly supported by our iReady data, which shows that
our interventions and instructional practices have successfully prevented students from falling significantly further behind.

e OSPI Report Card: The growth percentiles in Reading and Math, as reflected in our OSPI report card, further validate our
progress. These improvements indicate that we are closing achievement gaps and moving students closer to proficiency.

Leveraging Strengths for Areas of Improvement:

e Replicating Success Building-Wide: The key to leveraging these strengths lies in identifying and replicating the successful
practices and interventions that have been implemented in specific classrooms or grade levels. By scaling these effective
strategies across the entire school, we can ensure that all students benefit from the same level of support and instruction.

Focus on Consistency: To continue our progress, we need to focus on consistency in implementing best practices. Ensuring
that every classroom is using evidence-based instructional strategies will help us maintain and even accelerate the growth
we ve achieved.

Building on Momentum: The positive trends in our data indicate that we are on the right track. We can build on this
momentum by continuing to monitor student progress closely, using data to inform instruction, and making adjustments as
needed to support all students.

Targeted Professional Development: Providing targeted professional development to teachers, focusing on the areas where
we’ve seen the most success, will empower educators to apply these strategies consistently across all classrooms.

What are possible root causes your team has identified for areas of improvement? Consider of areas of strength and what it will take to build
strength in other areas.

Possible Root Causes Identified by Our Team:



Section 4: Comprehensive Needs Assessment (Component #1 - Needs Assessment Summary)

Gaps in Foundational Skills: Although we've seen growth in ELA and Math, students may still lack some foundational
skills, which hinders their ability to fully transfer this growth into measurable achievement. These gaps might be especially
pronounced among English Language Learners (ELLs), who require more targeted support to bridge these foundational gaps.

Inconsistent Application of Best Practices: While our instructional strategies have led to growth, there may be
inconsistencies in the application of best practices across different classrooms. Ensuring that all students have access to
high-quality, evidence-based instruction is crucial for converting growth into achievement.

Challenges with Differentiation: Differentiating instruction to meet the diverse needs of students is an ongoing challenge.
Some students may require more tailored support to reach their full potential, particularly those who are already showing
progress but need additional help to meet standardized benchmarks.

Student Engagement and Motivation: Low levels of student engagement and motivation, particularly among those who are
chronically absent, could be contributing to the gap between growth and achievement. This may also be linked to students not
feeling a strong sense of belonging within the school community, which can impact their overall academic performance. It
may also be linked to a sense of low self-efficacy around academic tasks.

Attendance and Chronic Absenteeism: Chronic absenteeism remains a significant barrier to student success. Students who
are frequently absent miss critical instruction time, making it difficult for them to keep up with their peers and fully
demonstrate their growth in standardized assessments.

Sense of Belonging: A lack of a strong sense of belonging among students may be influencing both their academic
performance and attendance. Students who do not feel connected to their school community are less likely to be motivated to
attend regularly and engage fully in their learning.

Next Steps:

e Targeted Interventions: Implement more targeted interventions to address gaps in foundational skills, particularly for ELLs
and other students who need additional support.

Professional Development: Provide professional development for teachers to ensure consistent application of best practices
across all classrooms and to enhance differentiation strategies.

Engagement Strategies: Develop and implement strategies to increase student engagement and motivation, with a focus on
creating a more inclusive and supportive school environment.



Section 4: Comprehensive Needs Assessment (Component #1 - Needs Assessment Summary)

Attendance Initiatives: Continue to explore and implement strategies to reduce chronic absenteeism, such as personalized
outreach, mentorship programs, and partnerships with families.

Sense of Belonging: Invest in programs and initiatives that foster a greater sense of belonging among students, which could
include community-building activities, student leadership opportunities, and culturally responsive teaching practices.

Educators

1. What professional learning and support have you identified that the school’s staff (e.g., administrators, educators, counselors,
paraprofessionals, support staff, etc.) need to strengthen the implementation of evidence-based practices for both teaching and learning, as
well as intervention supports (e.g., positive behavior interventions)?

Professional Learning and Support Needs Identified:

1. Differentiated Instruction Training:

o Need: Our educators would benefit from ongoing professional development focused on differentiated instruction
techniques. This is crucial to ensure that all students, particularly those who are struggling or excelling, receive the
tailored support they need to succeed.

Support Plan: We plan to bring in experts to provide workshops and coaching sessions that focus on how to effectively
differentiate instruction within a diverse classroom setting. This includes strategies for modifying content, process, and
product based on students’ readiness levels, interests, and learning profiles.

2. Positive Behavior Interventions and Supports



Section 4: Comprehensive Needs Assessment (Component #1 - Needs Assessment Summary)

o Need: To address behavioral challenges and enhance the school climate, staff members—including teachers,
paraprofessionals, and support staff—need training in implementing Positive Behavior Interventions and Supports
(PBIS) effectively.

Support Plan: We aim to provide a series of workshops on PBIS, focusing on proactive strategies for defining,
teaching, and supporting appropriate student behaviors to create a positive school environment. This training will also
include sessions on data-driven decision-making to track behavior trends and adjust interventions as needed.

3. Culturally Responsive Teaching:

o Need: As our student population becomes more diverse, there is a growing need for professional development in
culturally responsive teaching practices. This will help educators connect with students from different backgrounds
and create an inclusive classroom environment.

Support Plan: We plan to collaborate with cultural competence experts to offer training that equips staff with the skills
to recognize their own biases, understand students’ cultural contexts, and adapt their teaching methods to meet the
needs of all learners.

4. Data-Driven Instruction and Intervention:

o Need: To ensure that data is being used effectively to inform instruction and interventions, all staff members need
additional training on data analysis and interpretation. This includes understanding how to use assessment data to
identify students' needs, monitor progress, and adjust teaching strategies accordingly.

o Support Plan: We will implement ongoing professional learning communities (PLCs) where educators can collaborate
on data-driven practices. These PLCs will focus on analyzing student data, sharing best practices, and developing
action plans to support student achievement.

5. Social-Emotional Learning (SEL):

o Need: Given the increasing need to support students’ social-emotional well-being, professional learning in SEL is
essential for all staff. This will help educators and support staff better understand and respond to students' emotional
and psychological needs, which in turn supports their academic success.

o Support Plan: We plan to integrate SEL training into our professional development calendar, with sessions led by
school counselors and outside experts. The training will focus on strategies for incorporating SEL into daily
instruction, as well as how to recognize and address students’ social-emotional challenges.

2. What professional learning and support have you already implemented that is proving to be powerful and effective? What are your metrics
for identifying them as successful?



Section 4: Comprehensive Needs Assessment (Component #1 - Needs Assessment Summary)
Professional Learning and Support Implemented:

1. Master Instructional Schedule (Non-Negotiable):

o What We Did: We established a master instructional schedule that is non-negotiable, ensuring consistency and fidelity
in instructional delivery across all grade levels.

o Success Metrics: This schedule has allowed for uninterrupted instructional time and more focused teaching, leading to
improved student outcomes. We’ ve observed increased student engagement and a more structured learning
environment, contributing to the overall success of our instructional programs.

2. Collaborative Data Review and Planning:

o What We Did: We’ve dedicated time during our early release Fridays for collaborative teams to review their data and
plan accordingly. This time allows educators to analyze student progress, share strategies, and make data-informed
decisions to enhance instruction.

Success Metrics: The effectiveness of this approach is evident in the improved alignment of teaching practices across
classrooms and the targeted interventions that have resulted from these collaborative sessions. We’ ve seen measurable
growth in student achievement, as reflected in our growth percentiles in Reading and Math. Additionally, our
schoolwide Acceleration Block provides timely intervention for students in T2 and T3 — allowing us to also target our
students that are identified as MLL and receiving LAP services.

3. Common Planning Time for Grade-Level Teams:

o What We Did: We provided all grade-level teams with common planning time, ensuring that teachers have regular
opportunities to collaborate, plan lessons, and share resources.

o Success Metrics: This has led to greater consistency in instruction across grade levels and more cohesive teaching
strategies. The common planning time has also fostered stronger teamwork and professional collaboration, which are
reflected in the positive feedback from staff and the improved academic performance of students.

4. Strengthened School-Wide PBIS Supports:

o What We Did: We have enhanced our Positive Behavior Interventions and Supports (PBIS) by introducing several key
strategies:

« Calming Corners: Each classroom now includes a calming corner, providing students with a designated space
to regulate their emotions.
PAX Strategies: These strategies are used in common spaces to promote positive behavior.
ROAR Room: A dedicated space where students can self-identify their emotions and use tools to return to
baseline.



Section 4: Comprehensive Needs Assessment (Component #1 - Needs Assessment Summary)

« Wellness Room: Equipped with sensory and physical equipment, the Wellness Room is used by students after
unstructured time, like recess, to help them re-center and prepare for learning.

o Success Metrics: These initiatives have resulted in a noticeable reduction in behavioral incidents and an increase in
students’ ability to manage their emotions. The PBIS data shows a decrease in office referrals and an increase in
positive behavior recognition, indicating the success of these supports.

5. Expanded Counseling and Student Support Services:

o What We Did: We added a second counselor and a Student Support Advocate to bolster our efforts in small group
instruction and family support.

o Success Metrics: The impact of these additions is evident in the increased availability of support for students and
families. Small group interventions have become more effective, and there’s been a rise in family engagement and
communication. The additional support has also contributed to the overall improvement in student well-being and
academic achievement.

Systems of Support

1. Describe resource inequities (funding, staffing, materials, resources, etc.) identified through conducting the Comprehensive Needs
Assessment that will be addressed in this plan.

Resource Inequities Identified and Addressed:

Over the last three years, with the leadership of a new team, we have made significant strides in addressing the organizational and
technical aspects that were creating resource inequities within our school. These efforts have focused on ensuring that all students
have access to the high-quality education and support they need to succeed.

1. Expansion of the Dual Language Program:


Section 4: Comprehensive Needs Assessment (Component #1 - Needs Assessment Summary)

o Inequity Addressed: Previously, our Dual Language program faced challenges due to limited district-wide support and
resources, which affected its implementation and sustainability.

o Action Taken: We have successfully moved towards having our Dual Language program adopted as a district-wide
initiative. This has brought additional support and funding, allowing us to recruit and retain highly qualified
Spanish-speaking teachers. This expansion ensures that all students in the program have access to a consistent and
well-supported bilingual education.

2. Increased Social-Emotional Support:

o Inequity Addressed: Our school identified a gap in the social-emotional support available to students, with insufficient
counseling resources to meet the growing needs of our student population.

o Action Taken: In response, we have added a second counselor to our staff, significantly enhancing our capacity to
address students! social-emotional needs. Additionally, we have implemented a research-affirmed social-emotional
curriculum, which provides students with the tools and skills they need to manage their emotions, build healthy
relationships, and succeed academically.

3. Organizational and Technical Improvements:

o Inequity Addressed: The school previously struggled with organizational and technical challenges that created
inequities in access to resources, staffing, and materials.

o Action Taken: Through focused efforts, we have streamlined our organizational processes and improved the technical
infrastructure to ensure that all students and staff have equitable access to the resources they need. These
improvements have created a more equitable learning environment, where all students can thrive.

2. Consider the degree to which your school’s system of support is grounded in meeting the cultural, behavioral, social-emotional, and
academic needs of students: Identify areas of the strength for your school’s system of support and how other areas will be strengthened.

Strengths in Our School’s System of Support:

1. Cultural Responsiveness:

o Strength: Our expansion of the Dual Language program into a district-wide initiative is a significant strength in
meeting the cultural and linguistic needs of our students. By recruiting and retaining highly qualified Spanish-speaking
teachers, we ensure that students in the program receive culturally responsive instruction that honors and leverages
their bilingualism.

A SMARTIE Goal is specific, measurable, attainable, realistic, time-bound, inclusive, and equitable and should answer the questions:
What will be improved?

By how much?

By when?

And, for whom/what?

How will we know if progress toward the goal is being made?

When and how often will data be checked for progress?

Questions to ask and answer when addressing inclusion and equity:
Will achieving this goal build success and/or shrink disparity gaps for specific student groups in our learning community?
Does the goal ensure that traditionally marginalized or historically unserved/underserved students have equitable access, and is there an
element of fairness and justice inherent in the goal?
If the outcome specified in this goal isn’t specifically promoting equity and inclusion, is the process of achieving this goal going to improve
equity and inclusion for all students? How?
Who have we consulted to check for unintended negative consequences? Who needs to be consulted?
