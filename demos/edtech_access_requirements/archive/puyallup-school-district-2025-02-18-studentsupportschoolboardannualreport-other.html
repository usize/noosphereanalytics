Student Support Annual Report

Foster Care: The Student Support division partners with educators, foster parents, social workers, state
agencies, and advocates to support students in foster care by encouraging innovative practices that
reduce educational disruptions, strengthen school stability, and improve academic performance. Schools,
child welfare agencies, communities, and families work together to provide equitable opportunities,
specialized services, and useful supports that are essential for students in foster care to be successful in
school and in life. The number of students placed in foster care has remained stable in Puyallup School
District. Social worker, Therssa Warren, coordinates support for 62 students, down from 90 students
during the 2023-2024 school year. We anticipate continued stability with new reunification legislation.



McKinney-Vento (MV) Homelessness: Students are homeless if they lack a fixed, regular, and adequate
nighttime residence. The Student Support division ensures youth experiencing homelessness are
immediately enrolled and have educational stability. Puyallup SD MV numbers over the past 5 years have
continuously increased from 323 in 2020-2021 to 605 students thus far in the 2024-25 school year. We’ve
secured community partnerships with four new organizations to help facilitate access for families to
secure stable housing through Housing Connections, Helping Hand House, the REACH Center in Tacoma,
and Coffee Oasis. These partnerships have helped remove barriers for families struggling to get into stable
housing by removing first and last months rent fees; waiving application fees as well as other stipulations
that may prevent individuals from renting.

Suicidal Ideation/Self-Harm: Puyallup SD provides professional development, tools, and protocols for
school support staff to increase suicide awareness, prevention, and intervention. The data on suicidal
ideation and self-harm incidents in Puyallup School District shows a concerning trend over the past few
years, with an increase in documented incidents. In the 2020-2021 school year, there were 67 total
incidents, likely influenced by remote learning during the pandemic. This number grew significantly in the
following years, with 318 incidents reported in 2021-2022, 408 incidents in 2022-2023, and 495 incidents
documented through the 2023-2024 school year. As of January 31, 2025, there have been 342 incidents
documented in the current school year, suggesting a continued concern. It’s important to note that some
of these incidents are by the same students, highlighting the ongoing need for targeted intervention and
mental health support.

Chronic Absenteeism: Chronic absenteeism remains a significant focus for the Student Support division in
Puyallup School District, with efforts aimed at addressing both excused and unexcused absences. Students
who miss more than two days in a month are considered chronically absent or at risk. During the 2022-
2023 school year, 33% of students experienced chronic absenteeism, showing a 5% improvement from
38% in 2021-2022. To tackle this issue, the district has implemented comprehensive guidance, resources,
and training on truancy laws for all school office and administrative teams since the start of the 2024-
2025 school year. More than 100 truancy petitions were filed, and over 120 Student Attendance Success
Board (SASB) meetings were held in 2023-2024, leading to attendance improvements for 46% of students
involved. These efforts are showing positive outcomes, with a 5% increase in regular attendance in just
the past year, according to the most recent OSPI Report Card. While elementary schools are making more
rapid progress, plans are in place to provide more targeted support for secondary schools to continue
improving attendance across all grade levels.

Chronic absenteeism in Puyallup School District is influenced by a range of factors that affect students’
well-being. These include increased anxiety, depression, social isolation, excessive gaming, poor sleep
habits, transportation challenges, and substance abuse. These barriers not only affect students’
attendance but can also lead to psychological, social, and academic struggles that hinder their progress in
the general education curriculum. Over time, students may develop negative self-images, experience
dysfunctional relationships, and become disconnected from their school community. This detachment can
significantly interfere with on-time graduation, increasing the risk of students dropping out of school
altogether. Addressing these underlying issues is essential to improving attendance and supporting the
long-term success of students.

School Counselors and Social Workers:

School counselors, social workers, and SEL TOSAs are instrumental in supporting the social-emotional
well-being of students in Puyallup School District. Through comprehensive Social Emotional Learning (SEL)
programs, such as Second Step and Character Strong, they promote emotional regulation, goal setting,
relationship-building, and responsible decision-making across all grade levels. SEL TOSAs provide valuable
support at the building level, assisting in the development of behavior matrices, lesson plans, and
discipline referral processes. Social workers collaborate with families and external agencies to address
challenges such as homelessness and foster care, ensuring stability and reducing disruptions to students’
education. By fostering a supportive environment, these professionals help students navigate difficult
situations, manage mental health concerns, and build resilience. Their work is critical in combating issues
like chronic absenteeism, substance abuse, and suicidal ideation, while striving to improve overall student
success and well-being.

Priority goals/objectives:

e Continue to provide universal social emotional curricular support at all grade levels.

e Network and create additional community partnerships to respond to the behavioral health needs of
students. In the past year, new behavioral health partners include Charlie Health, Seneca, and Oasis.

e Enhance post-secondary transition support for students in grades 10 through 12 who are
experiencing homelessness.

e Establish a District Attendance Team to conduct a needs assessment and develop evidence-
based strategies that help schools effectively address chronic absenteeism and tardiness,
ensuring targeted interventions that support student engagement and attendance.


e Build capacity and support at the secondary level for staff to utilize the tools available to them
to address chronic absenteeism.

Accomplishments/Effectiveness/Evidence of Success:

e Provided 6 hours of professional development to all K-12 teachers on social emotional
strategies and practices, including a multi-tiered system of support on mental health.

e Hired a Secondary McKinney-Vento Homeless Liaison through a grant, with a focus on
providing tailored guidance and resources to help these students navigate their educational
and career paths beyond high school.

Challenges:

e Rising Mental Health Needs: The increasing rates of suicidal ideation, self-harm, anxiety, and
depression among students pose a significant challenge in addressing the social-emotional
well-being of students. The growing need for mental health services requires additional
resources and support for both students and staff.

e Chronic Absenteeism: Despite efforts, chronic absenteeism remains a persistent issue,
especially at the secondary level, where factors like social isolation, anxiety, and
disengagement are more pronounced. Addressing these challenges requires more targeted
interventions, particularly for older students.

e Homelessness and Foster Care: As the number of students experiencing homelessness continues
to rise, it’s crucial to ensure these students receive the academic and social-emotional
support needed to stay engaged in school. Similarly, students in foster care face unique
educational disruptions that require consistent, tailored support.

e Staff Capacity and Training: While progress has been made, there is still a need to build more
capacity at the secondary level for staff to effectively address attendance issues, behavioral
health, and social-emotional learning. This requires ongoing professional development and
resource allocation.

Opportunities:

e Community Partnerships: Expanding community partnerships with organizations like Charlie
Health, Seneca, and Oasis creates new opportunities for behavioral health support and
collaborative interventions. Building additional partnerships will further strengthen the
district’s capacity to meet student needs.

e SEL Curriculum Integration: Continuing to provide and expand the use of universal SEL curricula
across all grade levels is an opportunity to promote long-term emotional resilience, reduce
behavioral challenges, and support overall student success.

e Data-Driven Strategies: The establishment of a District Attendance Team and the ongoing
analysis of chronic absenteeism data present an opportunity to implement evidence-based
strategies that can create meaningful change in attendance patterns and student
engagement.

e Targeted Support: Enhancing post-secondary transition support for students experiencing
homelessness provides an opportunity to improve long-term educational outcomes for this
vulnerable population and reduce barriers to higher education and career readiness.


Moving Forward:
To enhance student support, we will continue to:

Equip classroom teachers with the necessary skills, knowledge, and resources to deliver universal
social-emotional learning (SEL) support effectively. This ensures that every student has access to
the tools they need for emotional growth and success in the classroom.

Prioritize supporting annual Community Resource Fairs and Family Engagement Nights, providing
families with valuable resources and fostering stronger connections between the school and the
community. These events are key in addressing the diverse needs of our students and their
families.

Build partnerships with outside behavioral health agencies, expanding access to critical support
services for students facing mental health challenges, and enhancing overall student well-being.
Maintain on-site drug and alcohol counseling at each secondary school site, ensuring that
students have access to vital substance use prevention and intervention services at no cost,
helping them stay on track academically and emotionally.

By advancing these initiatives, we aim to create a more supportive and resource-rich environment that
promotes the academic, social, and emotional success of every student.
