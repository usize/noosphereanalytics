Data Analysis Through a Racial Equity Lens
Prepared by Scott Whitbeck and Dr. Allison Deno, Teaching and Learning

Tukwila School District uses the following five questions to analyze data through the lens of racial equity,
followed by our responses and action steps to address the gaps of student opportunity and

achievement:

Who are the under-represented groups (federal subgroups) affected? What are the potential impacts?

Elementary Reading:

24


e Students who identify as Black or Latinx have the highest percentages of 2 or more
levels behind grade level in Reading.

e Students who identify as multiracial have the highest percentage scoring early on or mid
or above grade level in Reading.

e More than twice as many students who are multilingual learners score 2 or more grade
levels below in Reading.

e Secondary Reading:
e Students who identify as Latinx or Native Hawaiian Pacific Islander have the highest
percentage of 2 or more grade levels behind in reading.
e Students who identify as Asian have the highest percentage scoring early, on, or mid or
above grade level in reading.
e More than twice as many students who are multilingual learners score 2 or more grade
levels below in Reading.

e Elementary Math:
e Students who identify as Black, Latinx, or Native Hawaiian/Pacific Islander have the
highest percentage of students scoring 2 or more grades below in Math.
e Students who identify as white have the highest percentage of students scoring early on
or mid or above grade level in math.
e The percentage of multilingual learner students scoring two or more grade levels below
in reading is more than twice as many as non-Multilingual learner students in math.

e Secondary Math:
e Students who identify as Black, Latinx or Native Hawaiian Pacific/Islander have the
highest percentage of students scoring 2 or more grades below in Math.
e Students who identify as Asian have the highest percentage of students scoring early on
or mid or above grade level in math.

Potential Impacts:
e Students who are 2 or more grade levels below in reading and math in the above-
mentioned racial groups may not be able to meet grade level standards at the end of
the year.

Q2. Does this policy, program, practice or decision worsen existing disparities or produce other unintended
impacts?
e Current racial disparities at the end of the 2nd diagnostic for iReady are in alignment with past
practice.
e One positive outcome from the 2nd diagnostic window indicates that the previously observed
trend of students falling further behind with each successive grade level was not observed
during this window.

Q3. How have you intentionally involved stakeholders who are also members of the communities affected
by this policy, practice or decision?

25


Principals and district leadership recently participated in a professional learning session with
staff from iReady to delve deeper into their individual school iReady levels. A next step will be
for each principal to have a 1:1 session with district and iReady staff to update their school
improvement plans and celebrate individual student achievements. Principals would then lead
discussions with teachers to identify students who need focused instruction to accelerate their
growth in skills as indicated by the date. Principals would also facilitate sharing of data with
their families, seeking their input on what the school can do to help accelerate their child’s
progress.

Q4. What are the barriers to more equitable outcomes?

Inconsistent core instructional practices coupled with an overreliance on pull-out instructional
services.
Providing infrequent in-person coaching for teachers on effective instructional strategies.

Q5. How will you mitigate the negative impacts and address barriers?

The district wide Multi-Tiered Support Systems (MTSS) Team will utilize the learnings from the
Inclusionary Practices Project (IPP) to develop recommendations for strong core instruction,
particularly in literacy. A menu of high-impact teaching strategies will be developed and serve
as the centerpiece for professional learning focused on universal design for instruction (UDL)
during the 2024-2025 school year. One example of this type of professional support is to provide
supportive coaching to teachers on ways to differentiated instruction within the classroom,
minimizing times that students receive instruction elswehere.

Anew math curriculum for grades K-5 will be adopted after a rigorous pilot that stretched over
two school years. We will initiate a series of professional development sessions over the course
of 2024-2025 and beyond for teachers and school leaders in the effective use of the new
curricular materials.

Assessment data will continue to be analyzed using Tukwila’s racial equity questions, to
continue this cycle of data-based continuous improvement.
