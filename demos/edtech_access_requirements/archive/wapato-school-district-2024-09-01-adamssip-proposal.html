Student Populations

1. What key takeaways does your school have about how student groups are performing on state and locally determined, school-level,
indicators of learning and teaching success (e.g., Washington School Improvement Framework)?

We have observed improvements in several academic areas among various sub-groups, including English Language Learners (ELL),
low-income students, and the overall student body. These gains indicate progress in moving students away from the lowest
performance levels. However, it is noteworthy that the performance of students with disabilities and American Indian/Alaska Native
students has remained stagnant, without significant gains.

The positive trend of increased performance among ELL, low-income, and all student sub-groups has introduced new discrepancies.
As more students transition out of the lowest performance tiers, these shifts result in increased performance variance, presenting new
challenges related to achievement gaps that we must address.

Additionally, the WIDA (World-Class Instructional Design and Assessment) data shows substantial improvement, with a significant

number of students exiting the program. This reflects the effectiveness of our interventions and support strategies for ELL students,
facilitating their progress toward academic proficiency.

In summary, while the overall academic performance of several sub-groups has improved, we need to remain vigilant about the

emerging disparities that accompany these advancements. The performance of students with disabilities and American Indian/Alaska
Native students requires targeted interventions to ensure that all student groups benefit from our school improvement efforts. Moving
forward, our focus will be on addressing these new achievement gaps and continuing to support the academic progress of all students.

2. What are areas of strength your team identified? What data supported them and how can they be leveraged for areas of improvement?

Our data indicates a significant eight-fold increase in WIDA performance, suggesting substantial progress in our English language
acquisition programs. While student behavior has fluctuated, the overall data suggests that students generally feel safe at school and
are ready to learn.

The school improvement data presents a mixed picture. One grade level met its goals in both English Language Arts (ELA) and
mathematics. Fourth grade met its goal in ELA but narrowly missed in mathematics. Fifth grade performance remained stable with no
significant changes over the year.

In our WIDA programs, we have placed a strong emphasis on incorporating student voice and enhancing writing skills. This focus
has contributed to the notable improvement in WIDA performance. Moving forward, we will continue to prioritize these areas,
providing more comprehensive feedback to our staff to support ongoing growth and development.

In summary, while we have achieved significant gains in some areas, particularly in WIDA performance, other areas show room for

3. What are possible root causes your team has identified for areas of improvement? Consider of areas of strength and what it will take to build
strength in other areas.

Over the past two years, our areas of strength have been emphasized as key components of consistent experiences and opportunities at
Tier 1. We have formalized discipline and student behavior systems, but turnover and disruptions have had an unexpected and
inadequately compensated impact. As a result, staff experienced fatigue early in the spring.

This situation parallels what occurs in classrooms where students grow at different rates, increasing demand and variance. Our teams

have implemented a "walk to" model for interventions, similar to Solution Tree’s RTI approach. While this model has been beneficial
in addressing student needs, it has also heightened the demands on teams, creating tensions as teachers must share the workload.
Differences in competency among teachers have become more evident, underscoring the need for continued capacity building and
professionalism within teams.

To address these challenges, it is essential to focus on developing team capacity and fostering professionalism. A significant part of
this effort involves adult Social Emotional Learning (SEL), which will help staff improve their self-regulation skills. By prioritizing
adult SEL, we aim to create a more resilient and cohesive team environment, capable of effectively supporting student growth and
managing the increased demands of the intervention model.

In summary, while we have made strides in strengthening our Tier 1 elements and formalizing discipline systems, the impact of
turnover and disruptions has highlighted the need for better compensation mechanisms. The "walk to" intervention model has proven
effective but has also revealed underlying tensions and competency differences among teachers. Moving forward, our focus will be on
building team capacity and professionalism, with a particular emphasis on adult SEL to enhance staff self-regulation and overall team
cohesion. This approach will enable us to better manage the increased demands and variance associated with student growth and
ensure that our interventions are both effective and sustainable.

4. Title |, Part A, Targeted Assistance Program Requirement: Answer the following questions to satisfy the requirement of Component Two -
Identification of Students
a. Please describe how you select students for the program based on multiple criteria, e.g., Student Data, Teacher Referral, Previous
Placement, etc.

Students are placed using multiple criteria, primarily focusing on fluency and classroom-based assessments. This approach enables
the Professional Learning Community (PLC) to gather data and work with two distinct groups. The first group consists of
non-readers, identified through fluency scores, who require targeted interventions to develop reading skills. The second group is
determined by classroom assessments, which are based on critical targets identified by grade-level teams.

Staff sort students into these groups and design the necessary interventions to meet their specific needs. I provide oversight, meeting
every three weeks to review progress and ensure that interventions are on track, following a set task list. While larger-scale
benchmark assessments and Smarter Balanced Assessment (SBA) data points are also utilized to review the overall system, they lack
the timeliness and specificity needed for immediate intervention adjustments.

In summary, our placement process, driven by fluency and classroom assessments, allows us to effectively identify and address the
needs of non-readers and students requiring grade-level interventions. Regular oversight and timely data reviews ensure that our
intervention strategies remain responsive and effective.

b. Describe the process used to create the rank-order list of students identified to receive services, e.g., grade level, age, failing or at
risk of failing, not meeting standards, etc.

Our rank order lists are dynamic documents, continuously updated to reflect the greatest needs of our students and the critical targets
being addressed. Teams build these lists based on current data, ensuring that resources are allocated where they are most needed.
Groups focused on fluency receive enhanced staffing support with para-educators and interventionists to provide targeted assistance.

We conduct large-scale assessments three times a year, involving teachers who are directly engaged in daily instruction. These
assessments serve as a comprehensive evaluation tool, enabling us to track overall student performance and identify areas requiring
intervention. Based on classroom performance, students are adjusted and advanced as needed, with placement decisions made
collaboratively by the PLC group.

The large-scale assessments are crucial for confirming and supporting the real-time decisions made by PLC teams. These assessments
provide a broad perspective, validating the ongoing adjustments and ensuring that our instructional strategies are effective. This
integrated approach ensures that our educational interventions are data-driven and responsive to the evolving needs of our students.

Through continuous monitoring and dynamic adjustments, we strive to provide the best possible support for every student, promoting
sustained academic growth and success.

a. How will you use student data to monitor progress, at what intervals will you monitor progress, and how will student data be used
to determine if a student is ready to exit services?

To effectively monitor student progress, we employ a comprehensive strategy that leverages both large-scale benchmark exams and
regular data reviews by Professional Learning Community (PLC) teams. We administer three benchmark exams annually to evaluate
student performance on a broad scale. These exams provide critical data points that help us track progress towards key academic
standards and identify areas where students may need additional support.

In addition to these benchmarks, our PLC teams engage in weekly or bi-weekly data reviews. During these sessions, teachers and
staff analyze student performance data to monitor achievement, make informed decisions about placement in interventions, and
modify instructional strategies as needed. This frequent review cycle ensures that we can respond promptly to emerging trends and
address any issues before they become significant barriers to student success.

Subgroup data awareness is also growing within our teams, allowing us to tailor interventions more precisely to meet the diverse

needs of our student population. By disaggregating data, we can ensure that all subgroups are making adequate progress and receiving
the support they need.

Furthermore, all three grade levels use a "walk to" model, which facilitates targeted instruction by allowing students to move between
different groups based on their specific learning needs. This flexible approach enables us to provide more personalized and effective
interventions.

To determine if a student is ready to exit services, we consider multiple data points, including benchmark exam results, progress
monitoring data from PLC reviews, and classroom performance. A student is deemed ready to exit services when they consistently
demonstrate mastery of grade-level standards and show sustained academic growth. This multi-faceted approach ensures that
decisions are data-driven and focused on long-term student success.

For the 2024-2025 school year, we have identified multiple layers of support and professional learning activities to enhance the
implementation of evidence-based practices in both teaching and learning, as well as intervention supports. Our major and
overarching goal is to develop the Adams MTSS (Multi-Tiered System of Supports) map. This comprehensive, multi-level document
will delineate both academic and behavioral supports across tiers 1, 2, and 3 for all students.

As we construct this MTSS map, we will concurrently assess which components require additional training and determine which
areas already have sufficient training or guidance. The ultimate aim is to create an Adams Training Guide that will outlast current
staff and consistently benefit students, ensuring sustainable and systematic support.

A significant challenge we face is the recent turnover in our administrative team, with Adams currently experiencing its third assistant

principal in three years. This instability has disrupted our social skills programs, PBIS (Positive Behavioral Interventions and
Supports), and overall school culture. To address this, staff will need robust support to rebuild and reinforce routines that were lost
during these transitions. Our behavior data has shown a notable regression, indicating a critical need for targeted interventions to
restore and improve student behavior and school culture.

On the educational side, considerable energy will be devoted to supporting best practices in teaching, particularly through the
development and strengthening of Professional Learning Communities (PLCs) and the implementation of Response to Intervention
(RTI) strategies. This focus aligns with state efforts to revise goal rubrics and aid grade-level teams in designing effective
interventions for students. By fostering collaborative environments where educators can share insights and strategies, we aim to
enhance instructional quality and student outcomes.

Additionally, we will allocate resources to improve attendance and support newcomers. Attendance is a key indicator of student
engagement and success, and improving it will require a concerted effort from all staff members. For newcomers, particularly those
who are English language learners or from diverse cultural backgrounds, tailored support will be essential to ensure their smooth
integration and academic success.

transitions, we aim to support students’ social, emotional, and academic well-being as they move through different grade spans.

d. Are the students in your targeted assistance program able to participate in electives/enrichment time that pique their interest?
Yes, students in our targeted assistance program are afforded the opportunity to participate in electives and enrichment activities that
align with their interests. Whenever possible, we ensure that enrichment time is available for all students, including those receiving
targeted assistance. Students are given the opportunity to self-select from a range of enriching activities, allowing them to pursue their
passions and develop new skills outside of the core curriculum. This inclusive approach ensures that all students have access to
diverse learning opportunities that cater to their individual interests and talents.

7. Title |, Part A, Targeted Assistance Program Requirement: Answer the following questions to satisfy the requirement of Component 5 - Parent
and Family Engagement
a. How does your parent and family engagement strategy align to your targeted assistance practices and strategies?

Our parent and family engagement strategy aligns closely with our targeted assistance practices by actively involving families in
various activities within the school community. Through our targeted assistance program, we organize events such as math and
reading nights, as well as social-emotional learning (SEL) activities, which provide opportunities for families to participate in their
child's education. Additionally, we encourage family attendance at recognitions, school events like student of the month ceremonies,
annual award ceremonies, and student performances. By fostering a welcoming and inclusive environment, we aim to strengthen
partnerships between families and the school, ultimately supporting student success through collaborative efforts.

b. How will you evaluate your parent and family engagement strategies? How will you know if they are working?
To evaluate our parent and family engagement strategies, we will primarily assess attendance at school-related activities, including
math and reading nights, SEL activities, student recognitions, and performance events. Traditionally, we have seen high turnout for
these events, indicating a level of engagement within our parent and family community. Additionally, we will monitor conference
attendance, which has also been consistently high.

However, to gauge the effectiveness of our strategies more comprehensively, we recognize the need to delve deeper into the
qualitative aspects of engagement. This may involve soliciting feedback from parents and families through surveys or focus groups to
understand their experiences and perceptions of our engagement efforts. We will also assess the extent to which our strategies foster
