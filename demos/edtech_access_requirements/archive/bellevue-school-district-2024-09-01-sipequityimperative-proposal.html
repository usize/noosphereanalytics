High School Advisory & Student Efficacy

Our improvement strategies directly support our equity imperative by enhancing teaching quality, fostering inclusive learning environments, and prioritizing student voice. Professional development focused on
Domain 3 of the Danielson Framework for Teaching equips teachers with tools to ensure all students feel valued, engaged, and supported through purposeful communication, inclusive discussions, and
responsive assessments. Professional Learning Communities (PLCs) build on this by emphasizing student voice and agency, creating a shift toward flexible, student-centered classrooms where students actively
engage in meaningful work. Additionally, co-teaching models in Math and English foster inclusion by enabling students with IEP, 504, and MLL designations to access grade-level content alongside peers,
promoting a shared sense of belonging and academic success.

Staff Professional Development (Framework for Teaching)

Danielson Framework for Teaching: Domain 3 will be used to frame our professional development arc for the year.

Focusing staff professional development on Domain 3 in the Framework can significantly support Newport High School's equity imperative. By honing teachers' abilities to communicate purpose and content (3a),
they can ensure that students understand the relevance of their learning, helping students feel valued and included. Using questioning and discussion techniques (3b) encourages student voice, creating spaces
where all students, particularly those with IEP, 504, and MLL designations, feel heard and engaged. Engaging students in learning (3c) is central to fostering a sense of belonging, as students are more likely to feel
connected to a community that involves them in meaningful activities. Additionally, utilizing assessment for learning (3d) and responding flexibly to student needs (3e) ensures that instruction is responsive and
tailored, directly aligning with the goal of providing support and promoting agency for all students. This comprehensive approach not only enhances academic achievement but also cultivates a school
environment where every student feels empowered.

Professional Learning Communities (PLCs)

Last year, seven groups dedicated their professional learning to enhancing student voice and agency, and we anticipate a similar number will continue this work in the upcoming year. A review of last year's
learning and growth revealed that a focus on student voice and agency during collaborative time had a significant impact on classroom practices. Teachers demonstrated increased flexibility and responsiveness
by actively seeking and incorporating student feedback into lessons. Moreover, there was a shift in designing academic tasks to ensure that students are engaged in meaningful work, carrying out the cognitive lift
themselves. These efforts have contributed to more dynamic and student-centered learning environments across our classrooms.

Co-Teaching Models (Math and English)

Co-teaching fosters an inclusive learning environment where all students can learn from one another and appreciate diverse strengths. This model supports students with 504 plans, IEPs, and MLL needs by
providing them with access to grade-level content in the least restrictive environment. Through co-teaching, we create a sense of belonging and ensure that every student sees themselves as a general education
student capable of success. This approach promotes equity, engagement, and shared learning, aligning with our commitment to support the diverse needs of all learners. We currently provide co-teaching in
math for IEPs and English for both IEP and MLL language support.

Our three key improvement strategies aim to foster inclusivity and promote culturally responsive learning environments.

Culturally responsive teaching will involve professional development for staff, focusing on the VABB (Validate, Affirm, Build, Bridge) framework to recognize and value students’ diverse cultural backgrounds. By
revising cell phone management procedures, we will create a focused, distraction-free learning environment that enhances student engagement.

Additionally, implementing 10-minute mini-lessons on self-management skills will equip students with essential tools for academic and personal success. Together, these strategies deepen engagement and
ensure that all students have the opportunity to thrive in an equitable learning environment.

Culturally Responsive Teaching and Learning

The strategy for making teaching and learning more culturally-responsive involves two key phases:

Foundational Phase: This begins with full-day professional development (PD) sessions in August and October, led by Dr. Sharokky Hollie. These workshops cover essential topics such as creating culturally
responsive classrooms, identifying cultural behaviors, using the VABB (Validate, Affirm, Build, Bridge) framework, culturally responsive classroom management, and instructional strategies.

Building Phase: Throughout the school year, staff will engage in ongoing professional development during Wednesday professional development sessions, focusing on VABB clinics. In small groups, staff will
practice and deepen their understanding of culturally responsive teaching strategies.

This phased approach aims to build staff capacity and enhance culturally responsive teaching practices.

Use of Technology and Cell Phones

To minimize distractions and deepen student learning, we will revise our procedures for managing cell phones by implementing new guidelines and supports to eliminate phone usage during class and throughout
the school day. These revamped procedures aim to create an environment that maximizes student engagement and focus, ensuring that technology is used intentionally for learning purposes, rather than
becoming a source of distraction. The updated approach is designed to foster a more focused, productive classroom atmosphere, contributing to improved academic outcomes.

Social-Emotional Learning: Self-Management

We will implement 10-minute mini-lessons every Thursday to explicitly teach social-emotional and executive functioning skills, with a focus on self-management. These targeted lessons will cover essential 21st-
century skills such as organization, time management, task initiation, sustained attention, planning and sequencing steps, collaboration, interpersonal skills, critical thinking, problem solving, note-taking, study
strategies, and effective use of technology. By incorporating these brief lessons into the weekly schedule, we aim to equip students with practical tools to enhance their self-management, both academically and
personally.

Focal Students

Each educator will select one to two focal students who are long-term MLLs (6, 7, or 8 years in EL) in alignment with our equity imperative. Focal students help teachers develop the discipline of deeply
understanding the students’ needs, adjusting their teaching to meet those needs, and, in doing so, impacting the rest of the classroom as the quality of their teaching improves. Focal students reinforce an
orientation toward results. By setting specific goals for students and measuring progress toward these goals on a daily, weekly, and monthly basis, teachers gather concrete evidence about the effectiveness
of their teaching and how to make adjustments to better serve their students. Focal students help teachers make the subtle but critical shift from, “How well did | teach it?” to “How well did they learn it?”
With focal students in mind, teaching is not just executing a well-designed lesson, it’s checking to ensure that the students achieved the objectives of that lesson. In addition, focal students give teachers a
critical window into the factors beyond academics that influence learning. With sustained attention to focal students’ learning and a determined effort toward results, the opportunity and access gaps for
historically underserved students and families can be narrowed and closed.

Building Engagement and Sense of Belonging

Tyee will provide all staff with high quality professional learning centered around the following inquiry question: How are we creating conditions for increased student engagement and belonging? Our
learning will be centered on strategies to foster an inclusive school environment and meet students’ needs that have traditionally been pushed to the margins. Our belief is that if we are able to meet
students’ needs that have traditionally been pushed to the margins, we are meeting all Tyee student needs. Our key improvement strategies are:

* Building a Common Understanding of Engagement: During our monthly professional learning sessions, we will focus on building a common understanding of engagement and lesson elements that
contribute to authentic student engagement in learning. We will build connections between student engagement and knowledge of students to provide learning opportunities for students to connect who
they are to the content they are learning.

¢ Learning Walks: To further support learning, we will offer weekly learning walks to all staff focused on our inquiry question: “How are we creating conditions for increased student engagement and
belonging?” The goal of weekly learning walks is to provide staff with an opportunity to reflect on their own teaching practice. Learning walks will be held during a period of the school day. The offered period
will rotate week by week. Teachers with the selected period as planning can attend the learning walk. Staff will start the learning walk by meeting to discuss norms while in classrooms, and the note taking
document. Participating staff will visit 2 -— 3 classrooms for 10 minutes. While in the classroom teachers will be observing student actions related to engagement, noticing about the task, and content, and
teacher actions to invite student engagement. After visiting multiple classrooms, staff will meet back in the library to debrief noticings, wonderings, and celebrations.

* Collaborate to Calibrate: Learning walks will offer staff an opportunity to calibrate of effective teaching practices to build student engagement and sense of belonging.

The three strategies align with our equity imperative by focusing on creating an inclusive school environment that

fosters student engagement and belonging. By building a common understanding of engagement, educators can tailor lessons to connect with students’ identities, ensuring all students feel valued and
included. Learning walks provide a reflective practice for teachers to observe and discuss effective engagement strategies, promoting continuous improvement. Collaborating to calibrate teaching practices
ensures consistency in fostering a sense of belonging and meeting the needs of marginalized students, ultimately supporting the achievement of all students.

instruction, the how of supporting the development of school skills and social emotional skills, and
the how of building a classroom community that supports and cares for all learners — as the focus
of our professional learning this year.

Second, we recognize that our students who receive special education services are not the only
students who are not yet reaching this aspirational statement. Clyde Hill students who are multi-

and understand what is happening at a classroom, school, or district level. Clyde Hill leadership
teams will work to strengthen family engagement and partnerships with our families of
multilingual learners building our knowledge and facility with language inclusive tools, resources
available to support our partnerships with multilingual families and students, and working in
partnership with community partners, to learn from our families and adjust the systems we have
control of to better serve the needs of our students and families
