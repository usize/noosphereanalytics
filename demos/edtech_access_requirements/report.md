# Trends

1. Hard anchors to federal and state law in purchase docs and MOUs

* Explicit citations to IDEA, Section 504, ADA, FERPA, Title VI/IX, GEPA §427, RCW 28A.155, and WAC 392-172A appear in contract language and scopes of work (Oak Harbor 2024-09-09; Stanwood-Camano 2024-08-20; Aberdeen 2024-02-20 & 2024-06-18; Blaine 2024-08-28; Monroe 2025-04-18; Meridian 2025-04-18).
* Some districts waive stricter local procurement when state/federal special-ed statutes control placement/services procurement (Stanwood-Camano 2024-04-18; Blaine 2025-04-18; Meridian 2025-04-18).

2. District-wide policies now mandate accessible electronic resources, not just “best effort”

* Policy and procedure updates require website, LMS, teacher pages, social media, and online lectures to be accessible, with named owners for compliance questions (Federal Way 2025-03-25; Oak Harbor 2024-04-17; Eastmont 2025-02-10; Kennewick 2025-01-22; Puyallup 2025-04-01).
* Operations work shows concrete remediation steps like converting PDFs to HTML and making “accessible PDFs” when needed (Puyallup 2025-04-01).

3. Accessibility integrated into program selection and instructional materials reviews

* Rubrics include multiple modalities, differentiation, assistive-tech compatibility, and equitable access requirements (Meridian 2024-01-01 & 2024-03-20; Monroe 2024-03-27 & 2024-05-29; Aberdeen 2024-03-19 & 2024-04-16; Chewelah 2024-03-20 → 2024-06-18).
* UDL is named as the design baseline, with reported outcomes on inclusion (Kennewick 2024-10-23; Meridian 2024-03-06 resolution).

4. Services and contracts embed IEP/504 duties and assistive technology support

* Contracts require provision aligned to IEP/504, LRE, and related services, including training on devices and confidentiality (Oak Harbor 2024-03-06; Washougal 2024-08-27; Edmonds 2024-09-01).
* Grants and budgets target adaptive tech, apps, and staff training (Everett 2024-02-13; Bellingham 2025-10-01 memo).

5. Equity and representation targets extend accessibility beyond disability status

* District plans track participation for students with IEPs, MLLs, and other special populations in advanced coursework and programs, tying access to policy and procurement priorities (Tukwila 2024-01-09 & 2024-10-08; Bellevue 2024-09-01; Snohomish 2024-10-23; Mount Baker 2024-08-20; Blaine 2024-09-01).
* Bond and capital language includes ADA building improvements alongside tech upgrades (Puyallup 2025-02-18).

6. Rapid growth of digital assessment elevates compatibility requirements

* Districts highlight the jump in digital exams and the need to coordinate with Special Services/AT to meet diverse needs (Puyallup 2025-03-17; Chewelah 2024-08-28; Pasco 2024-01-14).

7. Generative AI access framed as an equity obligation

* Policies state maintaining equitable student access to GenAI tools, bounded by vendor restrictions and age appropriateness (Federal Way 2025-03-25; Eastmont 2025-02-10).

# What this means for edtech procurement

Minimum compliance bar vendors must meet, based on the records:

* Legal conformance: IDEA, Section 504, ADA, Title VI/IX, FERPA; WA RCW 28A.155; WAC 392-172A; for federal grants, GEPA §427.
* Product accessibility: Accessible web content for all public-facing and instructional use cases, including teacher pages and recordings. Expect districts to ask for written conformance statements and named escalation contacts.
* Instructional fit: Evidence of UDL support, multimodal access, and built-in accommodations.
* Assessment compatibility: Supports state and vendor digital testing with accessibility features.
* AT integration and training: Clear support for screen readers, captioning, input alternatives, and staff training plans.
* Data protection: FERPA-compliant handling for any student data in platforms and integrations.

# Practical buyer checklist (derived from the documents)

Ask vendors to provide, in writing, before award:

* Legal compliance attestation covering IDEA/504/ADA/FERPA/Title VI/IX; state RCW/WAC where applicable.
* Accessibility conformance report for web-delivered components; acceptance that district pages, teacher content, recordings, and LMS embeds must be accessible.
* UDL feature matrix: multiple means of representation, engagement, and expression; text-to-speech, captions, alt text workflows, keyboard nav, high-contrast and font scaling.
* IEP/504 alignment: how accommodations map to platform settings, per-student profiles, and audit logs.
* Assessment mode: proctoring compatibility, accommodations toggles, and documented support for statewide testing platforms.
* AT interoperability: screen reader support, captions, STT/TTs, eye-gaze or switch inputs if relevant; staff and family training artifacts.
* Data governance: FERPA data maps, role-based access, audit trails, and DPA addendum language.
* PDF/HTML plan: methods to avoid inaccessible PDFs and to remediate legacy documents.
* Named accessibility owner: a vendor contact for remediation SLAs.

# Risk signals to watch in bids

* Generic “ADA compliant” claims without scope or test evidence.
* No plan for teacher-generated content accessibility within the platform.
* Lack of per-student accommodation persistence across courses and assessments.
* Inability to document FERPA-aligned data flows and deletion.
* No training deliverables for AT or inclusion practices.
